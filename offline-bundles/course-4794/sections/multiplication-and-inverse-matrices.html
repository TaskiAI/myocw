Lecture 3: Multiplication and inverse matrices

Matrix Multiplication

We discuss four different ways of thinking about the product AB=CAB = CAB=C of two matrices. If AAA is an m×nm \times nm×n matrix and BBB is an n×pn \times pn×p matrix, then CCC is an m×pm \times pm×p matrix. We use cijc_{ij}cij​ to denote the entry in row iii and column jjj of matrix CCC.

Standard (row times column)

The standard way of describing a matrix product is to say that cijc_{ij}cij​ equals the dot product of row iii of matrix AAA and column jjj of matrix BBB. In other words,

cij=∑k=1naikbkj.c_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n a_{ik}b_{kj}.cij​=k=1∑n​aik​bkj​.

Columns

The product of matrix AAA and column jjj of matrix BBB equals column jjj of matrix CCC. This tells us that the columns of CCC are combinations of columns of AAA.

Rows

The product of row iii of matrix AAA and matrix BBB equals row iii of matrix CCC. So the rows of CCC are combinations of rows of BBB.

Column times row

A column of AAA is an m×1m \times 1m×1 vector and a row of BBB is a 1×p1 \times p1×p vector. Their product is a matrix:

[234][16]=[212318424]\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 12 \\ 3 & 18 \\ 4 & 24 \end{bmatrix}​234​​[1​6​]=​234​121824​​

The columns of this matrix are multiples of the column of AAA and the rows are multiples of the row of BBB. If we think of the entries in these rows as the coordinates (2,12)(2,12)(2,12) or (3,18)(3,18)(3,18) or (4,24)(4,24)(4,24), all these points lie on the same line; similarly for the two column vectors. Later we'll see that this is equivalent to saying that the row space of this matrix is a single line, as is the column space.

The product of AAA and BBB is the sum of these "column times row" matrices:

AB=∑k=1n[a1k⋮amk][bk1⋯bkn].AB = \sum_{k=1}^n \begin{bmatrix} a_{1k} \\ \vdots \\ a_{mk} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_{k1} & \cdots & b_{kn} \end{bmatrix}.AB=k=1∑n​​a1k​⋮amk​​​[bk1​​⋯​bkn​​].

Blocks

If we subdivide AAA and BBB into blocks that match properly, we can write the product AB=CAB = CAB=C in terms of products of the blocks:

[A1A2A3A4][B1B2B3B4]=[C1C2C3C4]\begin{bmatrix} A_1 & A_2 \\ A_3 & A_4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} B_1 & B_2 \\ B_3 & B_4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} C_1 & C_2 \\ C_3 & C_4 \end{bmatrix}[A1​A3​​A2​A4​​][B1​B3​​B2​B4​​]=[C1​C3​​C2​C4​​]

Here C1=A1B1+A2B3C_1 = A_1B_1 + A_2B_3C1​=A1​B1​+A2​B3​.

Inverses

Square matrices

If AAA is a square matrix, the most important question you can ask about it is whether it has an inverse A−1A^{-1}A−1. If it does, then A−1A=I=AA−1A^{-1}A = I = AA^{-1}A−1A=I=AA−1 and we say that AAA is invertible or nonsingular.

If AAA is singular – i.e. AAA does not have an inverse – its determinant is zero and we can find some non-zero vector x\mathbf{x}x for which Ax=0A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}Ax=0. For example:

[1326][3−1]=[00]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}[12​36​][3−1​]=[00​]

In this example, three times the first column minus one times the second column equals the zero vector; the two column vectors lie on the same line.

Finding the inverse of a matrix is closely related to solving systems of linear equations:

[1327][acbd]=[1001]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}[12​37​][ab​cd​]=[10​01​]

Here, the first matrix is AAA, the second is A−1A^{-1}A−1, and the third is III.

can be read as saying "A times column jjj of A−1A^{-1}A−1 equals column jjj of the identity matrix". This is just a special form of the equation Ax=bA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b.

Gauss-Jordan Elimination

We can use the method of elimination to solve two or more linear equations at the same time. Just augment the matrix with the whole identity matrix III:

[13∣1027∣01]→[13∣1001∣−21]→[10∣7−301∣−21].\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & | & 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 7 & | & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & | & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & | & -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & | & 7 & -3 \\ 0 & 1 & | & -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}.[12​37​∣∣​10​01​]→[10​31​∣∣​1−2​01​]→[10​01​∣∣​7−2​−31​].

(Once we have used Gauss' elimination method to convert the original matrix to upper triangular form, we go on to use Jordan's idea of eliminating entries in the upper right portion of the matrix.)

A−1=[7−3−21].A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & -3 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}.A−1=[7−2​−31​].

As in the last lecture, we can write the results of the elimination method as the product of a number of elimination matrices EijE_{ij}Eij​ with the matrix AAA. Letting EEE be the product of all the EijE_{ij}Eij​, we write the result of this Gauss-Jordan elimination using block matrices:

E[AI]=[IE].E \left[ \begin{array}{c|c} A & I \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c|c} I & E \end{array} \right].E[A​I​]=[I​E​].

But if EA=IEA = IEA=I, then E=A−1E = A^{-1}E=A−1.
