Lecture Notes

Independence, basis, and dimension

What does it mean for vectors to be independent? How does the idea of independence help us describe subspaces like the nullspace?

Linear independence

Suppose AAA is an mmm by nnn matrix with m<nm < nm<n (so Ax=bAx = bAx=b has more unknowns than equations). AAA has at least one free variable, so there are nonzero solutions to Ax=0Ax = 0Ax=0. A combination of the columns is zero, so the columns of this AAA are dependent.

We say vectors x1,x2,…xnx_1, x_2, \dots x_nx1​,x2​,…xn​ are linearly independent (or just independent) if c1x1+c2x2+⋯+cnxn=0c_1 x_1 + c_2 x_2 + \dots + c_n x_n = 0c1​x1​+c2​x2​+⋯+cn​xn​=0 only when c1,c2,…,cnc_1, c_2, \dots, c_nc1​,c2​,…,cn​ are all 000. When those vectors are the columns of AAA, the only solution to Ax=0Ax = 0Ax=0 is x=0x = 0x=0.

Two vectors are independent if they do not lie on the same line. Three vectors are independent if they do not lie in the same plane. Thinking of AxAxAx as a linear combination of the column vectors of AAA, we see that the column vectors of AAA are independent exactly when the nullspace of AAA contains only the zero vector.

If the columns of AAA are independent then all columns are pivot columns, the rank of AAA is nnn, and there are no free variables. If the columns of AAA are dependent then the rank of AAA is less than nnn and there are free variables.

Spanning a space

Vectors v1,v2,…vkv_1, v_2, \dots v_kv1​,v2​,…vk​ span a space when the space consists of all combinations of those vectors. For example, the column vectors of AAA span the column space of AAA.

If vectors v1,v2,…vkv_1, v_2, \dots v_kv1​,v2​,…vk​ span a space SSS, then SSS is the smallest space containing those vectors.

Basis and dimension

A basis for a vector space is a sequence of vectors v1,v2,…vdv_1, v_2, \dots v_dv1​,v2​,…vd​ with two properties:

  • v1,v2,…vdv_1, v_2, \dots v_dv1​,v2​,…vd​ are independent
  • v1,v2,…vdv_1, v_2, \dots v_dv1​,v2​,…vd​ span the vector space.

The basis of a space tells us everything we need to know about that space.

Example: R3\mathbb{R}^3R3

One basis for R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 is (100),(010),(001)\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}​100​​,​010​​,​001​​. These are independent because:

c1[100]+c2[010]+c3[001]=[000]c_1 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + c_2 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + c_3 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}c1​​100​​+c2​​010​​+c3​​001​​=​000​​

is only possible when c1=c2=c3=0c_1 = c_2 = c_3 = 0c1​=c2​=c3​=0. These vectors span R3\mathbb{R}^3R3.

As discussed at the start of Lecture 10, the vectors (112)\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}​112​​, (225)\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}​225​​ and (338)\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 3 \\ 8 \end{pmatrix}​338​​ do not form a basis for R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 because these are the column vectors of a matrix that has two identical rows. The three vectors are not linearly independent.

In general, nnn vectors in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn form a basis if they are the column vectors of an invertible matrix.

Basis for a subspace

The vectors (112)\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}​112​​ and (225)\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}​225​​ span a plane in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 but they cannot form a basis for R3\mathbb{R}^3R3. Given a space, every basis for that space has the same number of vectors; that number is the dimension of the space. So there are exactly nnn vectors in every basis for Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn.

Bases of a column space and nullspace

Suppose:

A=[123111211231]A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix}A=​111​212​323​111​​

By definition, the four column vectors of AAA span the column space of AAA. The third and fourth column vectors are dependent on the first and second, and the first two columns are independent. Therefore, the first two column vectors are the pivot columns. They form a basis for the column space C(A)C(A)C(A). The matrix has rank 2. In fact, for any matrix AAA we can say:

rank(A)=number of pivot columns of A=dimension of C(A).rank(A) = \text{number of pivot columns of } A = \text{dimension of } C(A).rank(A)=number of pivot columns of A=dimension of C(A).

(Note that matrices have a rank but not a dimension. Subspaces have a dimension but not a rank.)

The column vectors of this AAA are not independent, so the nullspace N(A)N(A)N(A) contains more than just the zero vector. Because the third column is the sum of the first two, we know that the vector (−110)\begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}​−110​​ is in the nullspace. Similarly, (0−110)\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}​0−110​​ is also in N(A)N(A)N(A). These are the two special solutions to Ax=0Ax = 0Ax=0. We'll see that:

dimension of N(A)=number of free variables=n−r,\text{dimension of } N(A) = \text{number of free variables} = n - r,dimension of N(A)=number of free variables=n−r,

so we know that the dimension of N(A)N(A)N(A) is 4−2=24 - 2 = 24−2=2. These two special solutions form a basis for the nullspace.

Problem 9.2: (3.5 #20.) Find a basis for the plane x−2y+3z=0x - 2y + 3z = 0x−2y+3z=0 in R3R^3R3. Then find a basis for the intersection of that plane with the xyxyxy plane. Then find a basis for all vectors perpendicular to the plane.
