Session Overview

Like differential equations, Markov matrices describe changes over time. Once again, the eigenvalues and eigenvectors describe the long term behavior of the system. In this session we also learn about Fourier series, which describe periodic functions as points in an infinite dimensional vector space.

Markov matrices; Fourier series

In this lecture we look at Markov matrices and Fourier series – two applications of eigenvalues and projections.

Eigenvalues of ATA^TAT

The eigenvalues of AAA and the eigenvalues of ATA^TAT are the same:

(A−λI)T=AT−λI(A - \lambda I)^T = A^T - \lambda I(A−λI)T=AT−λI,

so property 10 of determinants tells us that det⁡(A−λI)=det⁡(AT−λI)\det(A - \lambda I) = \det(A^T - \lambda I)det(A−λI)=det(AT−λI). If λ\lambdaλ is an eigenvalue of AAA then det⁡(AT−λI)=0\det(A^T - \lambda I) = 0det(AT−λI)=0 and λ\lambdaλ is also an eigenvalue of ATA^TAT.

Markov matrices

A matrix like:

A=(.1.01.3.2.99.3.70.4)A = \begin{pmatrix} .1 & .01 & .3 \\ .2 & .99 & .3 \\ .7 & 0 & .4 \end{pmatrix}A=​.1.2.7​.01.990​.3.3.4​​

in which all entries are non-negative and each column adds to 1 is called a Markov matrix. These requirements come from Markov matrices' use in probability. Squaring or raising a Markov matrix to a power gives us another Markov matrix.

When dealing with systems of differential equations, eigenvectors with the eigenvalue 0 represented steady states. Here we're dealing with powers of matrices and get a steady state when λ=1\lambda = 1λ=1 is an eigenvalue.

The constraint that the columns add to 1 guarantees that 1 is an eigenvalue. All other eigenvalues will be less than 1. Remember that (if AAA has nnn independent eigenvectors) the solution to uk=Aku0u_k = A^k u_0uk​=Aku0​ is uk=c1x1+c2λ2kx2+⋯+cnλnku_k = c_1 x_1 + c_2 \lambda_2^k x_2 + \cdots + c_n \lambda_n^kuk​=c1​x1​+c2​λ2k​x2​+⋯+cn​λnk​. If λ1=1\lambda_1 = 1λ1​=1 and all others eigenvalues are less than one the system approaches the steady state c1x1c_1 x_1c1​x1​. This is the x1x_1x1​ component of u0u_0u0​.

Why does the fact that the columns sum to 1 guarantee that 1 is an eigenvalue? If 1 is an eigenvalue of AAA, then:

A−1I=(−.9.01.3.2−.01.3.70−.6)A - 1I = \begin{pmatrix} -.9 & .01 & .3 \\ .2 & -.01 & .3 \\ .7 & 0 & -.6 \end{pmatrix}A−1I=​−.9.2.7​.01−.010​.3.3−.6​​

Fourier series

The key idea above was that the basis of vectors qiq_iqi​ was orthonormal. Fourier series are built on this idea. We can describe a function f(x)f(x)f(x) in terms of trigonometric functions:

f(x)=a0+a1cos⁡x+b1sin⁡x+a2cos⁡2x+b2sin⁡2x+⋯ .f(x) = a_0 + a_1 \cos x + b_1 \sin x + a_2 \cos 2x + b_2 \sin 2x + \cdots.f(x)=a0​+a1​cosx+b1​sinx+a2​cos2x+b2​sin2x+⋯.

This Fourier series is an infinite sum and the previous example was finite, but the two are related by the fact that the cosines and sines in the Fourier series are orthogonal.

We're now working in an infinite dimensional vector space. The vectors in this space are functions and the (orthogonal) basis vectors are 1,cos⁡x,sin⁡x,cos⁡2x,sin⁡2x,…1, \cos x, \sin x, \cos 2x, \sin 2x, \ldots1,cosx,sinx,cos2x,sin2x,…

What does "orthogonal" mean in this context? How do we compute a dot product or inner product in this vector space? For vectors in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn the inner product is vTw=v1w1+v2w2+⋯+vnwnv^T w = v_1 w_1 + v_2 w_2 + \cdots + v_n w_nvTw=v1​w1​+v2​w2​+⋯+vn​wn​. Functions are described by a continuum of values f(x)f(x)f(x) rather than by a discrete collection of components viv_ivi​. The best parallel to the vector dot product is:

fTg=∫02πf(x)g(x)dx.f^T g = \int_0^{2\pi} f(x)g(x)dx.fTg=∫02π​f(x)g(x)dx.

f(x)=f(x+2π)f(x) = f(x + 2\pi)f(x)=f(x+2π).
