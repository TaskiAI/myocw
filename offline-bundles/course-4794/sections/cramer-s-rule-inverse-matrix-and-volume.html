Cramer's rule, inverse matrix, and volume

We know a formula for and some properties of the determinant. Now we see how the determinant can be used.

Formula for A−1A^{-1}A−1

We know:

A−1=(abcd)−1=1ad−bc(d−b−ca)A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}A−1=(ac​bd​)−1=ad−bc1​(d−c​−ba​)

Can we get a formula for the inverse of a 3 by 3 or nnn by nnn matrix? We expect that det⁡A\det AdetA will be involved, as it is in the 2 by 2 example, and by looking at the cofactor matrix (d−b−ca)\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}(d−c​−ba​) we might guess that cofactors will be involved.

In fact:

A−1=1det⁡ACTA^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} C^TA−1=detA1​CT

where CCC is the matrix of cofactors – please notice the transpose! Cofactors of row one of AAA go into column 1 of A−1A^{-1}A−1, and then we divide by the determinant.

The determinant of AAA involves products with nnn terms and the cofactor matrix involves products of n−1n-1n−1 terms. AAA and 1det⁡ACT\frac{1}{\det A} C^TdetA1​CT might cancel each other. This is much easier to see from our formula for the determinant than when using Gauss-Jordan elimination. To more formally verify the formula, we'll check that ACT=(det⁡A)IAC^T = (\det A)IACT=(detA)I.

ACT=(a11…a1n⋮⋮an1…ann)(C11…Cn1⋮⋮C1n…Cnn)AC^T = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & \dots & a_{1n} \\ \vdots & & \vdots \\ a_{n1} & \dots & a_{nn} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} C_{11} & \dots & C_{n1} \\ \vdots & & \vdots \\ C_{1n} & \dots & C_{nn} \end{pmatrix}ACT=​a11​⋮an1​​……​a1n​⋮ann​​​​C11​⋮C1n​​……​Cn1​⋮Cnn​​​

The entry in the first row and first column of the product matrix is:

∑j=1na1jCj1=det⁡A.\sum_{j=1}^n a_{1j} C_{j1} = \det A.j=1∑n​a1j​Cj1​=detA.

(This is just the cofactor formula for the determinant.) This happens for every entry on the diagonal of ACTAC^TACT. To finish proving that ACT=(det⁡A)IAC^T = (\det A)IACT=(detA)I, we just need to check that the off-diagonal entries of ACTAC^TACT are zero. In the two by two case, multiplying the entries in row 1 of AAA by the entries in column 2 of CTC^TCT gives a(−b)+b(a)=0a(-b) + b(a) = 0a(−b)+b(a)=0. This is the determinant of As=(abab)A_s = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ a & b \end{pmatrix}As​=(aa​bb​). In higher dimensions, the product of the first row of AAA and the last column of CTC^TCT equals the determinant of a matrix whose first and last rows are identical. This happens with all the off diagonal matrices, which confirms that A−1=1det⁡ACTA^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} C^TA−1=detA1​CT.

This formula helps us answer questions about how the inverse changes when the matrix changes.

Cramer's Rule for x=A−1bx = A^{-1}bx=A−1b

We know that if Ax=bAx = bAx=b and AAA is nonsingular, then x=A−1bx = A^{-1}bx=A−1b. Applying the formula A−1=CT/det⁡AA^{-1} = C^T / \det AA−1=CT/detA gives us:

x=1det⁡ACTb.x = \frac{1}{\det A} C^T b.x=detA1​CTb.

Cramer's rule gives us another way of looking at this equation. To derive this rule we break xxx down into its components. Because the jjj'th component of CTbC^T bCTb is a sum of cofactors times some number, it is the determinant of some matrix BjB_jBj​.

xj=det⁡Bjdet⁡A,x_j = \frac{\det B_j}{\det A},xj​=detAdetBj​​,

where BjB_jBj​ is the matrix created by starting with AAA and then replacing column jjj with bbb, so:

B1=(blast n−1columnsof A)andBn=(first n−1columnsof Ab)B_1 = \begin{pmatrix} b & \begin{array}{c} \text{last } n-1 \\ \text{columns} \\ \text{of } A \end{array} \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad B_n = \begin{pmatrix} \begin{array}{c} \text{first } n-1 \\ \text{columns} \\ \text{of } A \end{array} & b \end{pmatrix}B1​=​b​last n−1columnsof A​​​andBn​=​first n−1columnsof A​​b​​

This agrees with our formula x1=det⁡B1det⁡Ax_1 = \frac{\det B_1}{\det A}x1​=detAdetB1​​. When taking the determinant of B1B_1B1​ we get a sum whose first term is b1b_1b1​ times the cofactor C11C_{11}C11​ of AAA. Computing inverses using Cramer's rule is usually less efficient than using elimination.

∣det⁡A∣|\det A|∣detA∣ = volume of box

Claim: ∣det⁡A∣|\det A|∣detA∣ is the volume of the box (parallelepiped) whose edges are the column vectors of AAA. (We could equally well use the row vectors, forming a different box with the same volume.) If A=IA = IA=I, then the box is a unit cube and its volume is 1. Because this agrees with our claim, we can conclude that the volume obeys determinant property 1. If A=QA = QA=Q is an orthogonal matrix then the box is a unit cube in a different orientation with volume 1=∣det⁡Q∣1 = |\det Q|1=∣detQ∣. (Because QQQ is an orthogonal matrix, QTQ=IQ^T Q = IQTQ=I and so det⁡Q=±1\det Q = \pm 1detQ=±1.) Swapping two columns of AAA does not change the volume of the box or (remembering that det⁡A=det⁡AT\det A = \det A^TdetA=detAT) the absolute value of the determinant (property 2). If we show that the volume of the box also obeys property 3 we'll have proven ∣det⁡A∣|\det A|∣detA∣ equals the volume of the box.

Figure 1: A 3D diagram showing vectors originating from the origin, forming the edges of a parallelepiped. The endpoints of two vectors are labeled (a11,a12,a13)(a_{11}, a_{12}, a_{13})(a11​,a12​,a13​) and (a21,a22,a23)(a_{21}, a_{22}, a_{23})(a21​,a22​,a23​). The axes are labeled x, y, z.

Figure 1: The box whose edges are the column vectors of AAA.

If we double the length of one column of AAA, we double the volume of the box formed by its columns. Volume satisfies property 3(a). Property 3(b) says that the determinant is linear in the rows of the matrix:

∣a+a′b+b′cd∣=∣abcd∣+∣a′b′cd∣\begin{vmatrix} a+a' & b+b' \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} a' & b' \\ c & d \end{vmatrix}​a+a′c​b+b′d​​=​ac​bd​​+​a′c​b′d​​

Figure 2 illustrates why this should be true.

Figure 2: A 2D diagram illustrating vector addition and the area property. Vectors (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) and (c,d)(c,d)(c,d) are shown. A third vector, (a+a′,b+b′)(a+a', b+b')(a+a′,b+b′), is shown as the sum of a vector parallel to (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) and another vector (a′,b′)(a',b')(a′,b′). The areas formed by these vectors (parallelograms) are depicted, demonstrating the linearity of area with respect to vector addition.

Figure 2: Volume obeys property 3(b).

Although it's not needed for our proof, we can also see that determinants obey property 4. If two edges of a box are equal, the box flattens out and has no volume.

Important note: If you know the coordinates for the corners of a box, then computing the volume of the box is as easy as calculating a determinant. In particular, the area of a parallelogram with edges [ab]\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}[ab​] and [cd]\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \end{bmatrix}[cd​] is ad−bcad - bcad−bc.

The area of a triangle with edges [ab]\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}[ab​] and [cd]\begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \end{bmatrix}[cd​] is half the area of that parallelogram, or 12(ad−bc)\frac{1}{2}(ad - bc)21​(ad−bc). The area of a triangle with vertices at (x1,y1)(x_1,y_1)(x1​,y1​), (x2,y2)(x_2,y_2)(x2​,y2​) and (x3,y3)(x_3,y_3)(x3​,y3​) is:

12∣x1y11x2y21x3y31∣\frac{1}{2} \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & y_1 & 1 \\ x_2 & y_2 & 1 \\ x_3 & y_3 & 1 \end{vmatrix}21​​x1​x2​x3​​y1​y2​y3​​111​​
