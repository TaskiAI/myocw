Method of Elimination

Elimination is the technique most commonly used by computer software to solve systems of linear equations. It finds a solution x\mathbf{x}x to Ax=bA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b whenever the matrix AAA is invertible. In the example used in class,

A=[121381041]andb=[2122]A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 8 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 12 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}A=​130​284​111​​andb=​2122​​

The number 1 in the upper left corner of AAA is called the first pivot. We recopy the first row, then multiply the numbers in it by an appropriate value (in this case 3) and subtract those values from the numbers in the second row. The first number in the second row becomes 0. We have thus eliminated the 3 in row 2 column 1.

The next step is to perform another elimination to get a 0 in row 3 column 1; here this is already the case.

The second pivot is the value 2 which now appears in row 2 column 2. We find a multiplier (in this case 2) by which we multiply the second row to eliminate the 4 in row 3 column 2. The third pivot is then the 5 now in row 3 column 3.

We started with an invertible matrix AAA and ended with an upper triangular matrix UUU; the lower left portion of UUU is filled with zeros. Pivots 1, 2, 5 are on the diagonal of UUU.

A=[121381041]→U=[12102−2005]A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 8 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \rightarrow \quad U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix}A=​130​284​111​​→U=​100​220​1−25​​

We repeat the multiplications and subtractions with the vector b=[2122]\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 12 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}b=​2122​​.

For example, we multiply the 2 in the first position by 3 and subtract from 12 to get 6 in the second position. When calculating by hand we can do this efficiently by augmenting the matrix AAA, appending the vector b\mathbf{b}b as a fourth or final column. The method of elimination transforms the equation Ax=bA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b into a new equation Ux=cU\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{c}Ux=c. In the example above,

U=[12102−2005]andc=[26−10]comes from bU = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{c} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 6 \\ -10 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{comes from } \mathbf{b}U=​100​220​1−25​​andc=​26−10​​comes from b

The equation Ux=cU\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{c}Ux=c is easy to solve by back substitution; in our example, z=−2z = -2z=−2, y=1y = 1y=1 and x=2x = 2x=2. This is also a solution to the original system Ax=bA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b.

The determinant of UUU is the product of the pivots. We will see this again. Pivots may not be 0. If there is a zero in the pivot position, we must exchange that row with one below to get a non-zero value in the pivot position. If there is a zero in the pivot position and no non-zero value below it, then the matrix AAA is not invertible. Elimination can not be used to find a unique solution to the system of equations – it doesn't exist.

Elimination Matrices

The product of a matrix (3x3) and a column vector (3x1) is a column vector (3x1) that is a linear combination of the columns of the matrix. The product of a row (1x3) and a matrix (3x3) is a row (1x3) that is a linear combination of the rows of the matrix.

We can subtract 3 times row 1 of matrix AAA from row 2 of AAA by calculating the matrix product:

[100−310001][121381041]=[12102−2041]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 8 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}​1−30​010​001​​​130​284​111​​=​100​224​1−21​​

The elimination matrix used to eliminate the entry in row mmm column nnn is denoted EmnE_{mn}Emn​. The calculation above took us from AAA to E21AE_{21}AE21​A. The three elimination steps leading to UUU were: E32(E31(E21A))=UE_{32}(E_{31}(E_{21}A)) = UE32​(E31​(E21​A))=U, where E31=IE_{31} = IE31​=I. Thus E32(E21A)=UE_{32}(E_{21}A) = UE32​(E21​A)=U. Matrix multiplication is associative, so we can also write (E32E21)A=U(E_{32}E_{21})A = U(E32​E21​)A=U. The product E32E21E_{32}E_{21}E32​E21​ tells us how to get from AAA to UUU. The inverse of the matrix E32E21E_{32}E_{21}E32​E21​ tells us how to get from UUU to AAA. If we solve Ux=EAx=EbU\mathbf{x} = EA\mathbf{x} = E\mathbf{b}Ux=EAx=Eb, then it is also true that Ax=bA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b. This is why the method of elimination works: all steps can be reversed.

A permutation matrix exchanges two rows of a matrix; for example,

P=[010100001]P = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}P=​010​100​001​​

The first and second rows of the matrix PAPAPA are the second and first rows of the matrix AAA. The matrix PPP is constructed by exchanging rows of the identity matrix. To exchange the columns of a matrix, multiply on the right (as in APAPAP) by a permutation matrix. Note that matrix multiplication is not commutative: PA≠APPA \neq APPA=AP.

Inverses

We have a matrix:

E21=[100−310001]E_{21} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}E21​=​1−30​010​001​​

which subtracts 3 times row 1 from row 2. To "undo" this operation we must add 3 times row 1 to row 2 using the inverse matrix:

E21−1=[100310001]E_{21}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}E21−1​=​130​010​001​​

In fact, E21−1E21=IE_{21}^{-1} E_{21} = IE21−1​E21​=I.
