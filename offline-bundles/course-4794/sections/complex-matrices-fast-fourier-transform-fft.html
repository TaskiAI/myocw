Lecture Notes

Complex matrices; fast Fourier transform

Matrices with all real entries can have complex eigenvalues! So we can't avoid working with complex numbers. In this lecture we learn to work with complex vectors and matrices.

The most important complex matrix is the Fourier matrix FnF_nFn​, which is used for Fourier transforms. Normally, multiplication by FnF_nFn​ would require n2n^2n2 multiplications. The fast Fourier transform (FFT) reduces this to roughly nlog⁡2nn \log_2 nnlog2​n multiplications, a revolutionary improvement.

Complex vectors

Length

Given a vector z=[z1z2⋮zn]∈Cnz = \begin{bmatrix} z_1 \\ z_2 \\ \vdots \\ z_n \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{C}^nz=​z1​z2​⋮zn​​​∈Cn with complex entries, how do we find its length? Our old definition:

zTz=[z1z2…zn][z1z2⋮zn]z^T z = \begin{bmatrix} z_1 & z_2 & \dots & z_n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} z_1 \\ z_2 \\ \vdots \\ z_n \end{bmatrix}zTz=[z1​​z2​​…​zn​​]​z1​z2​⋮zn​​​

is no good; this quantity isn't always positive. For example:

[1i][1i]=0.\begin{bmatrix} 1 & i \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ i \end{bmatrix} = 0.[1​i​][1i​]=0.

We don't want to define the length of [1i]\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ i \end{bmatrix}[1i​] to be 0. The correct definition is: ∣z∣2=zHz=∣z1∣2+∣z2∣2+⋯+∣zn∣2|z|^2 = z^H z = |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 + \dots + |z_n|^2∣z∣2=zHz=∣z1​∣2+∣z2​∣2+⋯+∣zn​∣2. Then we have:

(length [1i])2=[1−i][1i]=2.\left( \text{length } \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ i \end{bmatrix} \right)^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -i \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ i \end{bmatrix} = 2.(length [1i​])2=[1​−i​][1i​]=2.

To simplify our notation we write ∣z∣2=zHz|z|^2 = z^H z∣z∣2=zHz, where zH=zˉTz^H = \bar{z}^TzH=zˉT. The HHH comes from the name Hermite, and zHzz^H zzHz is read “z Hermitian z”.

Inner product

Similarly, the inner or dot product of two complex vectors is not just yTxy^T xyTx. We must also take the complex conjugate of yyy:

yHx=yˉTx=yˉ1x1+yˉ2x2+⋯+yˉnxn.y^H x = \bar{y}^T x = \bar{y}_1 x_1 + \bar{y}_2 x_2 + \dots + \bar{y}_n x_n.yHx=yˉ​Tx=yˉ​1​x1​+yˉ​2​x2​+⋯+yˉ​n​xn​.

Complex matrices

Hermitian matrices

Symmetric matrices are real valued matrices for which AT=AA^T = AAT=A. If AAA is complex, a nicer property is AH=AA^H = AAH=A; such a matrix is called Hermitian and we abbreviate AˉT\bar{A}^TAˉT as AHA^HAH. Note that the diagonal entries of a Hermitian matrix must be real. For example,

AH=A=[23+i3−i5].A^H = A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3+i \\ 3-i & 5 \end{bmatrix}.AH=A=[23−i​3+i5​].

Similar to symmetric matrices, Hermitian matrices have real eigenvalues and perpendicular eigenvectors.

Unitary matrices

What does it mean for complex vectors q1,q2,…,qnq_1, q_2, \dots, q_nq1​,q2​,…,qn​ to be perpendicular (or orthonormal)? We must use our new definition of the inner product. For a collection of qjq_jqj​ in complex space to be orthonormal, we require:

qjHqk={0j≠k1j=kq_j^H q_k = \begin{cases} 0 & j \ne k \\ 1 & j = k \end{cases}qjH​qk​={01​j=kj=k​

We can again define Q=[q1q2…qn]Q = \begin{bmatrix} q_1 & q_2 & \dots & q_n \end{bmatrix}Q=[q1​​q2​​…​qn​​], and then QHQ=IQ^H Q = IQHQ=I. Just as "Hermitian" is the complex equivalent of “symmetric”, the term “unitary” is analogous to "orthogonal”. A unitary matrix is a square matrix with perpendicular columns of unit length.

Discrete Fourier transform

A Fourier series is a way of writing a periodic function or signal as a sum of functions of different frequencies:

f(x)=a0+a1cos⁡x+b1sin⁡x+a2cos⁡2x+b2sin⁡2x+… .f(x) = a_0 + a_1 \cos x + b_1 \sin x + a_2 \cos 2x + b_2 \sin 2x + \dots.f(x)=a0​+a1​cosx+b1​sinx+a2​cos2x+b2​sin2x+….

When working with finite data sets, the discrete Fourier transform is the key to this decomposition.

In electrical engineering and computer science, the rows and columns of a matrix are numbered starting with 0, not 1 (and ending with n−1n-1n−1, not nnn). We'll follow this convention when discussing the Fourier matrix:

Fn=[111…11ww2…wn−11w2w4…w2(n−1)⋮⋮⋮⋱⋮1wn−1w2(n−1)…w(n−1)2]F_n = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & \dots & 1 \\ 1 & w & w^2 & \dots & w^{n-1} \\ 1 & w^2 & w^4 & \dots & w^{2(n-1)} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & w^{n-1} & w^{2(n-1)} & \dots & w^{(n-1)^2} \end{bmatrix}Fn​=​111⋮1​1ww2⋮wn−1​1w2w4⋮w2(n−1)​………⋱…​1wn−1w2(n−1)⋮w(n−1)2​​

Notice that Fn=FnˉF_n = \bar{F_n}Fn​=Fn​ˉ​ and (Fn)jk=wjk(F_n)_{jk} = w^{jk}(Fn​)jk​=wjk, where j,k=0,1,…,n−1j, k = 0, 1, \dots, n-1j,k=0,1,…,n−1 and the complex number www is w=ei⋅2π/nw = e^{i \cdot 2\pi/n}w=ei⋅2π/n (so wn=1w^n = 1wn=1). The columns of this matrix are orthogonal.

All the entries of FnF_nFn​ are on the unit circle in the complex plane, and raising each one to the nnn-th power gives 1. We could write w=cos⁡(2π/n)+isin⁡(2π/n)w = \cos(2\pi/n) + i \sin(2\pi/n)w=cos(2π/n)+isin(2π/n), but that would just make it harder to compute wjkw^{jk}wjk.

Because w4=1w^4 = 1w4=1 and w=e2πi/4=iw = e^{2\pi i/4} = iw=e2πi/4=i, our best example of a Fourier matrix is:

F4=[11111ii2i31i2i4i61i3i6i9]=[11111i−1−i1−11−11−i−1i]F_4 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & i & i^2 & i^3 \\ 1 & i^2 & i^4 & i^6 \\ 1 & i^3 & i^6 & i^9 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & i & -1 & -i \\ 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & -i & -1 & i \end{bmatrix}F4​=​1111​1ii2i3​1i2i4i6​1i3i6i9​​=​1111​1i−1−i​1−11−1​1−i−1i​​

To find the Fourier transform of a vector with four components (four data points) we multiply by F4F_4F4​.

It's easy to check that the columns of F4F_4F4​ are orthogonal, as long as we remember to conjugate when computing the inner product. However, F4F_4F4​ is not quite unitary because each column has length 2. We could divide each entry by 2 to get a matrix whose columns are orthonormal:

14F4HF4=I.\frac{1}{4} F_4^H F_4 = I.41​F4H​F4​=I.

An example

The signal corresponding to a single impulse at time zero is (roughly) described by [1000]\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}​1000​​. To find the Fourier transform of this signal we compute:

[11111i−1−i1−11−11−i−1i][1000]=[1111]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & i & -1 & -i \\ 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & -i & -1 & i \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}​1111​1i−1−i​1−11−1​1−i−1i​​​1000​​=​1111​​

A single impulse has all frequencies in equal amounts. If we multiply by F4F_4F4​ again we almost get back to (1,0,0,0)(1,0,0,0)(1,0,0,0):

[11111i−1−i1−11−11−i−1i][1111]=[4000]=4[1000]\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & i & -1 & -i \\ 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & -i & -1 & i \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = 4 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}​1111​1i−1−i​1−11−1​1−i−1i​​​1111​​=​4000​​=4​1000​​

Because FnF_nFn​ is unitary, multiplying by FnF_nFn​ and dividing by the scalar nnn inverts the transform.

Fast Fourier transform

Fourier matrices can be broken down into chunks with lots of zero entries; Fourier probably didn't notice this. Gauss did, but didn't realize how significant a discovery this was.

There's a nice relationship between FnF_nFn​ and F2nF_{2n}F2n​ related to the fact that w2nn=wnw_{2n}^n = w_nw2nn​=wn​:

F2n=[IDI−D][Fn00Fn]P,F_{2n} = \begin{bmatrix} I & D \\ I & -D \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} F_n & 0 \\ 0 & F_n \end{bmatrix} P,F2n​=[II​D−D​][Fn​0​0Fn​​]P,

where DDD is a diagonal matrix and PPP is a 2n2n2n by 2n2n2n permutation matrix:

P=[1000…000010…00⋮⋱⋮0000…100100…000001…00⋮⋱⋮0000…01]P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & & & & \ddots & & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & & & & \ddots & & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}P=​10⋮000⋮0​000100​010000​000010​……⋱………⋱…​001000​00⋮000⋮1​​

So, a 2n2n2n sized Fourier transform FFF times xxx which we might think would require (2n)2=4n2(2n)^2 = 4n^2(2n)2=4n2 operations can instead be performed using two size nnn Fourier transforms (2n22n^22n2 operations) plus two very simple matrix multiplications which require on the order of nnn multiplications. The matrix PPP picks out the even components x0,x2,x4,…x_0, x_2, x_4, \dotsx0​,x2​,x4​,… of a vector first, and then the odd ones – this calculation can be done very quickly.

Thus we can do a Fourier transform of size 64 on a vector by separating the vector into its odd and even components, performing a size 32 Fourier transform on each half of its components, then recombining the two halves through a process which involves multiplication by the diagonal matrix DDD.

D=[1ww2⋱wn−1]D = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & & & & \\ & w & & & \\ & & w^2 & & \\ & & & \ddots & \\ & & & & w^{n-1} \end{bmatrix}D=​1​w​w2​⋱​wn−1​​

Of course we can break each of those copies of F32F_{32}F32​ down into two copies of F16F_{16}F16​, and so on. In the end, instead of using n2n^2n2 operations to multiply by FnF_nFn​ we get the same result using about 12nlog⁡2n\frac{1}{2} n \log_2 n21​nlog2​n operations.

A typical case is n=1024=210n = 1024 = 2^{10}n=1024=210. Simply multiplying by FnF_nFn​ requires over a million calculations. The fast Fourier transform can be completed with only 12nlog⁡2n=5⋅1024\frac{1}{2} n \log_2 n = 5 \cdot 102421​nlog2​n=5⋅1024 calculations. This is 200 times faster!

This is only possible because Fourier matrices are special matrices with orthogonal columns. In the next lecture we'll return to dealing exclusively with real numbers and will learn about positive definite matrices, which are the matrices most often seen in applications.
