Column space and nullspace

In this lecture we continue to study subspaces, particularly the column space and nullspace of a matrix.

Review of subspaces

A vector space is a collection of vectors which is closed under linear combinations. In other words, for any two vectors vvv and www in the space and any two real numbers ccc and ddd, the vector cv+dwcv + dwcv+dw is also in the vector space. A subspace is a vector space contained inside a vector space.

A plane PPP containing

(000)\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}​000​​

and a line LLL containing

(000)\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}​000​​

are both subspaces of R3\mathbb{R}^3R3. The union P∪LP \cup LP∪L of those two subspaces is generally not a subspace, because the sum of a vector in PPP and a vector in LLL is probably not contained in P∪LP \cup LP∪L. The intersection S∩TS \cap TS∩T of two subspaces SSS and TTT is a subspace. To prove this, use the fact that both SSS and TTT are closed under linear combinations to show that their intersection is closed under linear combinations.

Column space of A

The column space of a matrix AAA is the vector space made up of all linear combinations of the columns of AAA.

Solving Ax = b

Given a matrix AAA, for what vectors bbb does Ax=bAx = bAx=b have a solution xxx?

Let

A=(112213314415)A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & 3 \\ 3 & 1 & 4 \\ 4 & 1 & 5 \end{pmatrix}A=​1234​1111​2345​​

Then Ax=bAx = bAx=b does not have a solution for every choice of bbb because solving Ax=bAx = bAx=b is equivalent to solving four linear equations in three unknowns. If there is a solution xxx to Ax=bAx = bAx=b, then bbb must be a linear combination of the columns of AAA. Only three columns cannot fill the entire four dimensional vector space — some vectors bbb cannot be expressed as linear combinations of columns of AAA.

Big question: what bbb's allow Ax=bAx = bAx=b to be solved? A useful approach is to choose xxx and find the vector b=Axb = Axb=Ax corresponding to that solution. The components of xxx are just the coefficients in a linear combination of columns of AAA. The system of linear equations Ax=bAx = bAx=b is solvable exactly when bbb is a vector in the column space of AAA.

For our example matrix AAA, what can we say about the column space of AAA? Are the columns of AAA independent? In other words, does each column contribute something new to the subspace? The third column of AAA is the sum of the first two columns, so does not add anything to the subspace. The column space of our matrix AAA is a two dimensional subspace of R4\mathbb{R}^4R4.

Problem 6.1:

(3.1 #30. Introduction to Linear Algebra: Strang) Suppose SSS and TTT are two subspaces of a vector space VVV.

a) Definition: The sum S+TS + TS+T contains all sums s+ts + ts+t of a vector sss in SSS and a vector ttt in TTT. Show that S+TS + TS+T satisfies the requirements (addition and scalar multiplication) for a vector space.

b) If SSS and TTT are lines in RmR^mRm, what is the difference between S+TS + TS+T and S∪TS \cup TS∪T? That union contains all vectors from SSS and TTT or both. Explain this statement: The span of S∪TS \cup TS∪T is S+TS + TS+T.

Solution:

a) Let s,s′s, s's,s′ be vectors in SSS, let t,t′t, t't,t′ be vectors in TTT, and let ccc be a scalar. Then (s+t)+(s′+t′)=(s+s′)+(t+t′)(s+t) + (s' + t') = (s + s') + (t + t')(s+t)+(s′+t′)=(s+s′)+(t+t′) and c(s+t)=cs+ctc(s + t) = cs + ctc(s+t)=cs+ct. Thus S+TS + TS+T is closed under addition and scalar multiplication; in other words, it satisfies the two requirements for a vector space.

b) If SSS and TTT are distinct lines, then S+TS + TS+T is a plane, whereas S∪TS \cup TS∪T is only the two lines. The span of S∪TS \cup TS∪T is the set of all combinations of vectors in this union of two lines. In particular, it contains all sums s+ts + ts+t of a vector sss in SSS and a vector ttt in TTT, and these sums form S+TS + TS+T. Since S+TS + TS+T contains both SSS and TTT, it contains S∪TS \cup TS∪T. Further, S+TS + TS+T is a vector space. So it contains all combinations of vectors in itself; in particular, it contains the span of S∪TS \cup TS∪T. Thus the span of S∪TS \cup TS∪T is S+TS + TS+T.
