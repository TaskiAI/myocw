Session Overview

Vectors are easier to understand when they’re described in terms of orthogonal bases. In addition, the Four Fundamental Subspaces are orthogonal to each other in pairs.

If A is a rectangular matrix, Ax = b is often unsolvable. The matrix ATA will help us find a vector x̂ that comes as close as possible to solving Ax = b.

Lecture Notes

Orthogonal vectors and subspaces

In this lecture we learn what it means for vectors, bases and subspaces to be orthogonal. The symbol for this is ⊥\perp⊥.

The "big picture" of this course is that the row space of a matrix is orthogonal to its nullspace, and its column space is orthogonal to its left nullspace.

The relationships between these subspaces and their dimensions are as follows:

  • The row space (dimension rrr) is orthogonal (⊥\perp⊥) to the nullspace (dimension n−rn-rn−r).
  • The column space (dimension rrr) is orthogonal (⊥\perp⊥) to the left nullspace N(AT)N(A^T)N(AT) (dimension m−rm-rm−r).

Orthogonal vectors

Orthogonal is just another word for perpendicular. Two vectors are orthogonal if the angle between them is 90 degrees. If two vectors are orthogonal, they form a right triangle whose hypotenuse is the sum of the vectors. Thus, we can use the Pythagorean theorem to prove that the dot product xTy=yTxx^T y = y^T xxTy=yTx is zero exactly when xxx and yyy are orthogonal. (The length squared ∣∣x∣∣2||x||^2∣∣x∣∣2 equals xTxx^T xxTx.)

Note that all vectors are orthogonal to the zero vector.

Orthogonal subspaces

Subspace SSS is orthogonal to subspace TTT means: every vector in SSS is orthogonal to every vector in TTT. The blackboard is not orthogonal to the floor; two vectors in the line where the blackboard meets the floor aren't orthogonal to each other.

In the plane, the space containing only the zero vector and any line through the origin are orthogonal subspaces. A line through the origin and the whole plane are never orthogonal subspaces. Two lines through the origin are orthogonal subspaces if they meet at right angles.

Nullspace is perpendicular to row space

The row space of a matrix is orthogonal to the nullspace, because Ax=0Ax = 0Ax=0 means the dot product of xxx with each row of AAA is 000. But then the product of xxx with any combination of rows of AAA must be 000.

The column space is orthogonal to the left nullspace of AAA because the row space of ATA^TAT is perpendicular to the nullspace of ATA^TAT.

In some sense, the row space and the nullspace of a matrix subdivide RnR^nRn into two perpendicular subspaces. For

A=[1252410]A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 2 & 4 & 10 \end{bmatrix}A=[12​24​510​]

the row space has dimension 1 and basis

[125]\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}​125​​

and the nullspace has dimension 2 and is the plane through the origin perpendicular to the vector

[125]\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}​125​​

Not only is the nullspace orthogonal to the row space, their dimensions add up to the dimension of the whole space. We say that the nullspace and the row space are orthogonal complements in RnR^nRn. The nullspace contains all the vectors that are perpendicular to the row space, and vice versa.

We could say that this is part two of the fundamental theorem of linear algebra. Part one gives the dimensions of the four subspaces, part two says those subspaces come in orthogonal pairs, and part three will be about orthogonal bases for these subspaces.

N(ATA)=N(A)N(A^T A) = N(A)N(ATA)=N(A)

Due to measurement error, Ax=bAx = bAx=b is often unsolvable if m>nm > nm>n. Our next challenge is to find the best possible solution in this case. The matrix ATAA^T AATA plays a key role in this effort: the central equation is ATAx^=ATbA^T A \hat{x} = A^T bATAx^=ATb.

We know that ATAA^T AATA is square (n×nn \times nn×n) and symmetric. When is it invertible?

Suppose

Then:

ATA=[111125][111215]=[38830]A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 8 \\ 8 & 30 \end{bmatrix}ATA=[11​12​15​]​111​125​​=[38​830​]

is invertible. ATAA^T AATA is not always invertible. In fact:

N(ATA)=N(A)N(A^T A) = N(A)N(ATA)=N(A)
rank of ATA=rank of A\text{rank of } A^T A = \text{rank of } Arank of ATA=rank of A

We conclude that ATAA^T AATA is invertible exactly when AAA has independent columns.

Problem 16.1: (4.1 #7. Introduction to Linear Algebra: Strang)

For every system of mmm equations with no solution, there are numbers y1,...,ymy_1, ..., y_my1​,...,ym​ that multiply the equations so they add up to 0=10 = 10=1. This is called Fredholm's Alternative:

Exactly one of these problems has a solution: Ax=bAx = bAx=b OR ATy=0A^T y = 0ATy=0 with yTb=1y^T b = 1yTb=1.

If bbb is not in the column space of AAA it is not orthogonal to the nullspace of ATA^TAT. Multiply the equations x1−x2=1x_1 - x_2 = 1x1​−x2​=1, x2−x3=1x_2 - x_3 = 1x2​−x3​=1 and x1−x3=1x_1 - x_3 = 1x1​−x3​=1 by numbers y1,y2y_1, y_2y1​,y2​ and y3y_3y3​ chosen so that the equations add up to 0=10 = 10=1.

Problem 16.2: (4.1#32.)

Suppose I give you four nonzero vectors r,n,cr, n, cr,n,c and lll in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2. a) What are the conditions for those to be bases for the four fundamental subspaces C(AT)C(A^T)C(AT), N(A)N(A)N(A), C(A)C(A)C(A), and N(AT)N(A^T)N(AT) of a 2 by 2 matrix? b) What is one possible matrix A?

Solution:

a) In order for rrr and nnn to be bases for N(A)N(A)N(A) and C(AT)C(A^T)C(AT), we must have

r⋅n=0,r \cdot n = 0,r⋅n=0,

as the row space and null space must be orthogonal. Similarly, in order for ccc and lll to form bases for C(A)C(A)C(A) and N(AT)N(A^T)N(AT) we need

c⋅l=0,c \cdot l = 0,c⋅l=0,

as the column space and the left nullspace are orthogonal. In addition, we need:
