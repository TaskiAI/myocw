Lecture Notes

Symmetric matrices and positive definiteness

Symmetric matrices are good – their eigenvalues are real and each has a complete set of orthonormal eigenvectors. Positive definite matrices are even better.

Symmetric matrices

A symmetric matrix is one for which A=ATA = A^TA=AT. If a matrix has some special property (e.g. it's a Markov matrix), its eigenvalues and eigenvectors are likely to have special properties as well. For a symmetric matrix with real number entries, the eigenvalues are real numbers and it's possible to choose a complete set of eigenvectors that are perpendicular (or even orthonormal).

If AAA has nnn independent eigenvectors we can write A=SAS−1A = SAS^{-1}A=SAS−1. If AAA is symmetric we can write A=QAQ−1=QAQTA = QAQ^{-1} = QAQ^TA=QAQ−1=QAQT, where QQQ is an an orthogonal matrix. Mathematicians call this the spectral theorem and think of the eigenvalues as the "spectrum" of the matrix. In mechanics it's called the principal axis theorem. In addition, any matrix of the form QAQTQAQ^TQAQT will be symmetric.

Real eigenvalues

Why are the eigenvalues of a symmetric matrix real? Suppose AAA is symmetric and Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx. Then we can conjugate to get Aˉxˉ=λˉxˉ\bar{A}\bar{x} = \bar{\lambda}\bar{x}Aˉxˉ=λˉxˉ. If the entries of AAA are real, this becomes Axˉ=λˉxˉA\bar{x} = \bar{\lambda}\bar{x}Axˉ=λˉxˉ. (This proves that complex eigenvalues of real valued matrices come in conjugate pairs.)

Now transpose to get xˉTAT=xˉTλˉ\bar{x}^T A^T = \bar{x}^T \bar{\lambda}xˉTAT=xˉTλˉ. Because AAA is symmetric we now have xˉTA=xˉTλˉ\bar{x}^T A = \bar{x}^T \bar{\lambda}xˉTA=xˉTλˉ. Multiplying both sides of this equation on the right by xxx gives:

xˉTAx=xˉTλˉx.\bar{x}^T A x = \bar{x}^T \bar{\lambda} x.xˉTAx=xˉTλˉx.

On the other hand, we can multiply Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx on the left by xˉT\bar{x}^TxˉT to get:

xˉTAx=xˉTλx.\bar{x}^T A x = \bar{x}^T \lambda x.xˉTAx=xˉTλx.

Comparing the two equations we see that xˉTAx=xˉTλˉx\bar{x}^T A x = \bar{x}^T \bar{\lambda} xxˉTAx=xˉTλˉx and, unless xˉTx\bar{x}^T xxˉTx is zero, we can conclude λ=λˉ\lambda = \bar{\lambda}λ=λˉ is real.

How do we know xˉTx≠0\bar{x}^T x \neq 0xˉTx=0?

If x≠0x \neq 0x=0 then xˉTx≠0\bar{x}^T x \neq 0xˉTx=0.

With complex vectors, as with complex numbers, multiplying by the conjugate is often helpful.

Symmetric matrices with real entries have A=ATA = A^TA=AT, real eigenvalues, and perpendicular eigenvectors. If AAA has complex entries, then it will have real eigenvalues and perpendicular eigenvectors if and only if A=A∗A = A^*A=A∗. (The proof of this follows the same pattern.)

Projection onto eigenvectors

If A=ATA = A^TA=AT, we can write:

A=QAQT=[q1q2⋯qn][λ1λ2⋱λn][q1Tq2T⋮qnT]A = QAQ^T = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{q}_1 & \mathbf{q}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{q}_n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & & & \\ & \lambda_2 & & \\ & & \ddots & \\ & & & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{q}_1^T \\ \mathbf{q}_2^T \\ \vdots \\ \mathbf{q}_n^T \end{bmatrix}A=QAQT=[q1​​q2​​⋯​qn​​]​λ1​​λ2​​⋱​λn​​​​q1T​q2T​⋮qnT​​​
=λ1q1q1T+λ2q2q2T+⋯+λnqnqnT= \lambda_1 \mathbf{q}_1 \mathbf{q}_1^T + \lambda_2 \mathbf{q}_2 \mathbf{q}_2^T + \cdots + \lambda_n \mathbf{q}_n \mathbf{q}_n^T=λ1​q1​q1T​+λ2​q2​q2T​+⋯+λn​qn​qnT​

The matrix qkqkT\mathbf{q}_k \mathbf{q}_k^Tqk​qkT​ is the projection matrix onto qk\mathbf{q}_kqk​, so every symmetric matrix is a combination of perpendicular projection matrices.

Information about eigenvalues

If we know that eigenvalues are real, we can ask whether they are positive or negative. (Remember that the signs of the eigenvalues are important in solving systems of differential equations.)

For very large matrices AAA, it's impractical to compute eigenvalues by solving ∣A−λI∣=0|A - \lambda I| = 0∣A−λI∣=0. However, it's not hard to compute the pivots, and the signs of the pivots of a symmetric matrix are the same as the signs of the eigenvalues:

number of positive pivots = number of positive eigenvalues.

Because the eigenvalues of A+bIA + bIA+bI are just bbb more than the eigenvalues of AAA, we can use this fact to find which eigenvalues of a symmetric matrix are greater or less than any real number bbb. This tells us a lot about the eigenvalues of AAA even if we can't compute them directly.

Positive definite matrices

A positive definite matrix is a symmetric matrix AAA for which all eigenvalues are positive. A good way to tell if a matrix is positive definite is to check that all its pivots are positive.

Let A=[5223]A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}A=[52​23​]. The pivots of this matrix are 5 and (det⁡A)/5=11/5(\det A)/5 = 11/5(detA)/5=11/5. The matrix is symmetric and its pivots (and therefore eigenvalues) are positive, so AAA is a positive definite matrix. Its eigenvalues are the solutions to:

∣A−λI∣=λ2−8λ+11=0,|A - \lambda I| = \lambda^2 - 8\lambda + 11 = 0,∣A−λI∣=λ2−8λ+11=0,

i.e. 4±54 \pm \sqrt{5}4±5​.

The determinant of a positive definite matrix is always positive but the determinant of [0−10−3]\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 0 & -3 \end{bmatrix}[00​−1−3​] is also positive, and that matrix isn't positive definite. If all of the subdeterminants of AAA are positive (determinants of the kkk by kkk matrices in the upper left corner of AAA, where 1≤k≤n1 \le k \le n1≤k≤n), then AAA is positive definite.

The subject of positive definite matrices brings together what we've learned about pivots, determinants and eigenvalues of square matrices. Soon we'll have a chance to bring together what we've learned in this course and apply it to non-square matrices.
