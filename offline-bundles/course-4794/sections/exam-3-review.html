Exam 3 covers the understanding of matrices through their eigenvalues. Elimination is history now, because it does NOT preserve eigenvalues. Notice the special types of matrices (like positive definite) and their factorizations.

Lecture Notes

Exam 3 review

The exam will cover the material through the singular value decomposition. Linear transformations and change of basis will be covered on the final. The main topics on this exam are:

  • Eigenvalues and eigenvectors
  • Differential equations dudt=Au\frac{du}{dt} = Audtdu​=Au and exponentials eAte^{At}eAt
  • Symmetric matrices A=ATA = A^TA=AT: These always have real eigenvalues, and they always have "enough" eigenvectors. The eigenvector matrix QQQ can be an orthogonal matrix, with A=QAQTA = QAQ^TA=QAQT.
  • Positive definite matrices
  • Similar matrices B=M−1AMB = M^{-1}AMB=M−1AM. Matrices AAA and BBB have the same eigenvalues; powers of AAA will "look like" powers of BBB.
  • Singular value decomposition

Sample problems

  1. This is a question about a differential equation with a skew symmetric matrix.

Suppose

dudt=Au=(0−1010−1010)u.\frac{du}{dt} = Au = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} u.dtdu​=Au=​010​−101​0−10​​u.

The general solution to this equation will look like

u(t)=c1eλ1tx1+c2eλ2tx2+c3eλ3tx3.u(t) = c_1e^{\lambda_1 t}x_1 + c_2e^{\lambda_2 t}x_2 + c_3e^{\lambda_3 t}x_3.u(t)=c1​eλ1​tx1​+c2​eλ2​tx2​+c3​eλ3​tx3​.

We might also notice that AAA is antisymmetric (AT=−AA^T = -AAT=−A) and realize that its eigenvalues will be imaginary. To find the other two eigenvalues, we'll solve the equation ∣A−λI∣=0|A - \lambda I| = 0∣A−λI∣=0.

∣−λ−101−λ−101−λ∣=−λ3−2λ=0.\begin{vmatrix} -\lambda & -1 & 0 \\ 1 & -\lambda & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & -\lambda \end{vmatrix} = -\lambda^3 - 2\lambda = 0.​−λ10​−1−λ1​0−1−λ​​=−λ3−2λ=0.

We conclude λ2=2i\lambda_2 = \sqrt{2}iλ2​=2​i and λ3=−2i\lambda_3 = -\sqrt{2}iλ3​=−2​i.

At this point we know that our solution will look like:

u(t)=c1x1+c2e2itx2+c3e−2itx3.u(t) = c_1x_1 + c_2e^{\sqrt{2}it} x_2 + c_3e^{-\sqrt{2}it}x_3.u(t)=c1​x1​+c2​e2​itx2​+c3​e−2​itx3​.

We can now see that the solution doesn't increase without bound or decay to zero. The size of eiθe^{i\theta}eiθ is the same for any θ\thetaθ; the exponentials here correspond to points on the unit circle.

b) The solution is periodic. When does it return to its original value? (What is its period?) This is not likely to be on the exam, but we can quickly remark that e2it=e0e^{\sqrt{2}it} = e^0e2​it=e0 when 2t=2π\sqrt{2}t = 2\pi2​t=2π, or when t=π2t = \pi\sqrt{2}t=π2​.

c) Show that two eigenvectors of AAA are orthogonal. The eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix or a skew symmetric matrix are always orthogonal. One choice of eigenvectors of AAA is:

x1=(101),x2=(−12i1),x3=(12i−1).x_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} , x_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ \sqrt{2}i \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} , x_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ \sqrt{2}i \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} .x1​=​101​​,x2​=​−12​i1​​,x3​=​12​i−1​​.

Don't forget to conjugate the first vector when computing the inner product of vectors with complex number entries.

d) The solution to this differential equation is u(t)=eAtu(0)u(t) = e^{At}u(0)u(t)=eAtu(0). How would we compute eAte^{At}eAt? If A=SAS−1A = SAS^{-1}A=SAS−1 then eAt=SeΛtS−1e^{At} = Se^{\Lambda t}S^{-1}eAt=SeΛtS−1 where

eΛt=(eλ1teλ2teλ3t).e^{\Lambda t} = \begin{pmatrix} e^{\lambda_1 t} & & \\ & e^{\lambda_2 t} & \\ & & e^{\lambda_3 t} \end{pmatrix} .eΛt=​eλ1​t​eλ2​t​eλ3​t​​.

So eAte^{At}eAt comes from the eigenvalues in Λ\LambdaΛ and the eigenvectors in SSS. Fact: A matrix has orthogonal eigenvectors exactly when AAT=ATAAA^T = A^TAAAT=ATA; i.e. when AAA commutes with its transpose. This is true of symmetric, skew symmetric and orthogonal matrices.

  1. We're told that a three by three matrix AAA has eigenvalues λ1=0\lambda_1 = 0λ1​=0, λ2=c\lambda_2 = cλ2​=c and λ3=2\lambda_3 = 2λ3​=2 and eigenvectors
x1=(111),x2=(1−10),x3=(11−2).x_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} , x_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} , x_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} .x1​=​111​​,x2​=​1−10​​,x3​=​11−2​​.

a) For which ccc is the matrix diagonalizable? The matrix is diagonalizable if it has 3 independent eigenvectors. Not only are x1,x2x_1, x_2x1​,x2​ and x3x_3x3​ independent, they're orthogonal. So the matrix is diagonalizable for all values of ccc.

b) For which values of ccc is the matrix symmetric? If A=QAQTA = QAQ^TA=QAQT is symmetric its eigenvalues (the entries of AAA) are real. On the other hand, if ccc is real, then AT=QΛTQ=AA^T = Q\Lambda^T Q = AAT=QΛTQ=A is symmetric. The matrix is symmetric for all real numbers ccc.

c) For which values of ccc is the matrix positive definite? All positive definite matrices are symmetric, so ccc must be real. The eigenvalues of a positive definite matrix must be positive. The eigenvalue 000 is not positive, so this matrix is not positive definite for any values of ccc. (If c>0c > 0c>0 then the matrix is positive semidefinite.)

d) Is it a Markov matrix? In a Markov matrix, one eigenvalue is 111 and the other eigenvalues are smaller than 111. Because λ3=2\lambda_3 = 2λ3​=2, this cannot be a Markov matrix for any value of ccc.

e) Could P=12AP = \frac{1}{2}AP=21​A be a projection matrix? Projection matrices are real and symmetric so their eigenvalues are real. In addition, we know that their eigenvalues are 111 and 000 because P2=PP^2 = PP2=P implies λ2=λ\lambda^2 = \lambdaλ2=λ. So AAA could be a projection matrix if c=0c = 0c=0 or c=2c = 2c=2. Note that it was the fact that the eigenvectors were orthogonal that made it possible to answer many of these questions.

Singular value decomposition (SVD) is a factorization

A=(orthogonal)(diagonal)(orthogonal)=UΣVT.A = (\text{orthogonal})(\text{diagonal})(\text{orthogonal}) = U\Sigma V^T.A=(orthogonal)(diagonal)(orthogonal)=UΣVT.

We can do this for any matrix AAA. The key is to look at the symmetric matrix ATA=VΣTΣVTA^TA = V\Sigma^T\Sigma V^TATA=VΣTΣVT; here VVV is the eigenvector matrix for ATAA^TAATA and ΣTΣ\Sigma^T\SigmaΣTΣ is the matrix of eigenvalues of ATAA^TAATA. Similarly, AAT=UΣΣTUTAA^T = U\Sigma\Sigma^TU^TAAT=UΣΣTUT and UUU is the eigenvector matrix for AATAA^TAAT. (Note that we can introduce a sign error if we're unlucky in choosing eigenvectors for the columns of UUU. To avoid this, use the formula Avi=σiuiAv_i = \sigma_i u_iAvi​=σi​ui​ to calculate UUU from VVV.) On the exam, you might be asked to find the SVD of a matrix AAA or you might be given information on UUU, Σ\SigmaΣ and VVV and asked about AAA.

  1. Suppose Σ=(3002)\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}Σ=(30​02​) and UUU and VVV each have two columns.

a) What can we say about AAA? We know AAA is a two by two matrix, and because U,VU, VU,V and Σ\SigmaΣ are all invertible we know AAA is nonsingular.

b) What if Σ=(300−5)\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & -5 \end{pmatrix}Σ=(30​0−5​)? This is not a valid possibility for Σ\SigmaΣ. The singular values – the diagonal entries of Σ\SigmaΣ – are never negative in a singular value decomposition.

c) What if Σ=(3000)\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}Σ=(30​00​)? Then AAA is a singular matrix of rank 1 and its nullspace has dimension 1. The four fundamental subspaces associated with AAA are spanned by orthonormal bases made up of selected columns of UUU and VVV. In this example, the second column of VVV is a basis for the nullspace of AAA.

  1. We're told that AAA is symmetric and orthogonal.

a) What can we say about its eigenvalues? The eigenvalues of symmetric matrices are real. The eigenvalues of orthogonal matrices QQQ have ∣λ∣=1|\lambda| = 1∣λ∣=1; multiplication by an orthogonal matrix doesn't change the length of a vector. So the eigenvalues of AAA can only be 111 or −1-1−1.

b) True or false: AAA is sure to be positive definite. False - it could have an eigenvalue of −1-1−1, as in

(100−1).\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} .(10​0−1​).

c) True or false: AAA has no repeated eigenvalues. False – if AAA is a three by three matrix or larger, it's guaranteed to have repeated eigenvalues because every λ\lambdaλ is 111 or −1-1−1.

d) Is AAA diagonalizable? Yes, because all symmetric and all orthogonal matrices can be diagonalized. In fact, we can choose the eigenvectors of AAA to be orthogonal.

e) Is AAA nonsingular? Yes; orthogonal matrices are all nonsingular.

f) Show P=12(A+I)P = \frac{1}{2}(A + I)P=21​(A+I) is a projection matrix. We could check that PPP is symmetric and that P2=PP^2 = PP2=P:

P2=(12(A+I))2=14(A2+2A+I).P^2 = \left(\frac{1}{2}(A+I)\right)^2 = \frac{1}{4}(A^2+2A+I).P2=(21​(A+I))2=41​(A2+2A+I).

Because AAA is orthogonal and symmetric, A2=ATA=IA^2 = A^TA = IA2=ATA=I, so

P2=14(A2+2A+I)=14(I+2A+I)=14(2A+2I)=12(A+I)=P.P^2 = \frac{1}{4}(A^2+2A+I) = \frac{1}{4}(I+2A+I) = \frac{1}{4}(2A+2I) = \frac{1}{2}(A+I) = P.P2=41​(A2+2A+I)=41​(I+2A+I)=41​(2A+2I)=21​(A+I)=P.

Or we could note that since the eigenvalues of AAA are 111 and −1-1−1 then the eigenvalues of 12(A+I)\frac{1}{2}(A+I)21​(A+I) must be 111 and 000.
