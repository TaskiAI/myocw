Properties of determinants

Determinants

Now halfway through the course, we leave behind rectangular matrices and focus on square ones. Our next big topics are determinants and eigenvalues. The determinant is a number associated with any square matrix; we'll write it as det⁡A\det AdetA or ∣A∣|A|∣A∣. The determinant encodes a lot of information about the matrix; the matrix is invertible exactly when the determinant is non-zero.

Properties

Rather than start with a big formula, we'll list the properties of the determinant. We already know that

∣abcd∣=ad−bc;\begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = ad - bc;​ac​bd​​=ad−bc;

these properties will give us a formula for the determinant of square matrices of all sizes.

  1. det⁡I=1\det I = 1detI=1
  2. If you exchange two rows of a matrix, you reverse the sign of its determinant from positive to negative or from negative to positive.
  3. (a) If we multiply one row of a matrix by ttt, the determinant is multiplied by ttt:
∣tatbcd∣=t∣abcd∣\begin{vmatrix} ta & tb \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = t \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix}​tac​tbd​​=t​ac​bd​​

(b) The determinant behaves like a linear function on the rows of the matrix:

∣a+a′b+b′cd∣=∣abcd∣+∣a′b′cd∣\begin{vmatrix} a+a' & b+b' \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} a' & b' \\ c & d \end{vmatrix}​a+a′c​b+b′d​​=​ac​bd​​+​a′c​b′d​​

Property 1 tells us that ∣1001∣=1\begin{vmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = 1​10​01​​=1. Property 2 tells us that ∣0110∣=−1\begin{vmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = -1​01​10​​=−1. The determinant of a permutation matrix PPP is 111 or −1-1−1 depending on whether PPP exchanges an even or odd number of rows. From these three properties we can deduce many others:

  1. If two rows of a matrix are equal, its determinant is zero.

This is because of property 2, the exchange rule. On the one hand, exchanging the two identical rows does not change the determinant. On the other hand, exchanging the two rows changes the sign of the determinant. Therefore the determinant must be 000.

  1. If i≠ji \neq ji=j, subtracting ttt times row iii from row jjj doesn't change the determinant.

In two dimensions, this argument looks like:

∣abc−tad−tb∣=∣abcd∣+∣ab−ta−tb∣property 3(b)=∣abcd∣−t∣abab∣property 3(a)=∣abcd∣property 4.\begin{align*} \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c-ta & d-tb \end{vmatrix} &= \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} + \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ -ta & -tb \end{vmatrix} \quad \text{property 3(b)} \\ &= \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} -t \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ a & b \end{vmatrix} \quad \text{property 3(a)} \\ &= \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} \quad \text{property 4.} \end{align*}​ac−ta​bd−tb​​​=​ac​bd​​+​a−ta​b−tb​​property 3(b)=​ac​bd​​−t​aa​bb​​property 3(a)=​ac​bd​​property 4.​

The proof for higher dimensional matrices is similar.

  1. If AAA has a row that is all zeros, then det⁡A=0\det A = 0detA=0. We get this from property 3 (a) by letting t=0t = 0t=0.
  2. The determinant of a triangular matrix is the product of the diagonal entries (pivots) d1,d2,…,dnd_1, d_2, \dots, d_nd1​,d2​,…,dn​.

Property 5 tells us that the determinant of the triangular matrix won't change if we use elimination to convert it to a diagonal matrix with the entries did_idi​ on its diagonal. Then property 3 (a) tells us that the determinant of this diagonal matrix is the product d1d2…dnd_1 d_2 \dots d_nd1​d2​…dn​ times the determinant of the identity matrix. Property 1 completes the argument. Note that we cannot use elimination to get a diagonal matrix if one of the did_idi​ is zero. In that case elimination will give us a row of zeros and property 6 gives us the conclusion we want.

  1. det⁡A=0\det A = 0detA=0 exactly when AAA is singular.

If AAA is singular, then we can use elimination to get a row of zeros, and property 6 tells us that the determinant is zero. If AAA is not singular, then elimination produces a full set of pivots d1,d2,…,dnd_1, d_2, \dots, d_nd1​,d2​,…,dn​ and the determinant is d1d2…dn≠0d_1 d_2 \dots d_n \neq 0d1​d2​…dn​=0 (with minus signs from row exchanges). We now have a very practical formula for the determinant of a non-singular matrix. In fact, the way computers find the determinants of large matrices is to first perform elimination (keeping track of whether the number of row exchanges is odd or even) and then multiply the pivots:

∣abcd∣→∣ab0d−cab∣,if a≠0, so\begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} \rightarrow \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & d - \frac{c}{a}b \end{vmatrix}, \quad \text{if } a \neq 0, \text{ so}​ac​bd​​→​a0​bd−ac​b​​,if a=0, so
∣abcd∣=a(d−cab)=ad−bc.\begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = a \left(d - \frac{c}{a}b\right) = ad - bc.​ac​bd​​=a(d−ac​b)=ad−bc.
  1. det⁡AB=(det⁡A)(det⁡B)\det AB = (\det A) (\det B)detAB=(detA)(detB)

This is very useful. Although the determinant of a sum does not equal the sum of the determinants, it is true that the determinant of a product equals the product of the determinants.

For example:

det⁡A−1=1det⁡A\det A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A}detA−1=detA1​

because A−1A=IA^{-1}A = IA−1A=I. (Note that if AAA is singular then A−1A^{-1}A−1 does not exist and det⁡A−1\det A^{-1}detA−1 is undefined.) Also, det⁡A2=(det⁡A)2\det A^2 = (\det A)^2detA2=(detA)2 and det⁡(2A)=2ndet⁡A\det (2A) = 2^n \det Adet(2A)=2ndetA (applying property 3 to each row of the matrix). This reminds us of volume - if we double the length, width and height of a three dimensional box, we increase its volume by a multiple of 23=82^3 = 823=8.

  1. det⁡AT=det⁡A\det A^T = \det AdetAT=detA
∣acbd∣=∣abcd∣=ad−bc.\begin{vmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = ad - bc.​ab​cd​​=​ac​bd​​=ad−bc.

This lets us translate properties (2, 3, 4, 5, 6) involving rows into statements about columns. For instance, if a column of a matrix is all zeros then the determinant of that matrix is zero. To see why ∣AT∣=∣A∣|A^T| = |A|∣AT∣=∣A∣, use elimination to write A=LUA = LUA=LU. The statement becomes ∣UTLT∣=∣LU∣|U^T L^T| = |LU|∣UTLT∣=∣LU∣. Rule 9 then tells us ∣UT∣∣LT∣=∣L∣∣U∣|U^T||L^T| = |L||U|∣UT∣∣LT∣=∣L∣∣U∣. Matrix LLL is a lower triangular matrix with 1's on the diagonal, so rule 5 tells us that ∣L∣=∣LT∣=1|L| = |L^T| = 1∣L∣=∣LT∣=1. Because UUU is upper triangular, rule 5 tells us that ∣U∣=∣UT∣|U| = |U^T|∣U∣=∣UT∣. Therefore ∣UT∣∣LT∣=∣L∣∣U∣|U^T||L^T| = |L||U|∣UT∣∣LT∣=∣L∣∣U∣ and ∣AT∣=∣A∣|A^T| = |A|∣AT∣=∣A∣.

We have one loose end to worry about. Rule 2 told us that a row exchange changes the sign of the determinant. If it's possible to do seven row exchanges and get the same matrix you would by doing ten row exchanges, then we could prove that the determinant equals its negative. To complete the proof that the determinant is well defined by properties 1, 2 and 3 we'd need to show that the result of an odd number of row exchanges (odd permutation) can never be the same as the result of an even number of row exchanges (even permutation).
