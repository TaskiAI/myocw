The Geometry of Linear Equations

Session Overview

A major application of linear algebra is to solving systems of linear equations. This lecture presents three ways of thinking about these systems. The “row method” focuses on the individual equations, the “column method” focuses on combining the columns, and the “matrix method” is an even more compact and powerful way of describing systems of linear equations.

Lecture Notes

The geometry of linear equations

The fundamental problem of linear algebra is to solve nnn linear equations in nnn unknowns; for example:

2x−y=0−x+2y=3.\begin{align*} 2x - y &= 0 \\ -x + 2y &= 3. \end{align*}2x−y−x+2y​=0=3.​

In this first lecture on linear algebra we view this problem in three ways. The system above is two dimensional (n=2n = 2n=2). By adding a third variable zzz we could expand it to three dimensions.

Row Picture

Plot the points that satisfy each equation. The intersection of the plots (if they do intersect) represents the solution to the system of equations. Looking at Figure 1 we see that the solution to this system of equations is x=1,y=2x = 1, y = 2x=1,y=2.

!session1-fig1 Figure 1: The lines 2x−y=02x - y = 02x−y=0 and −x+2y=3-x + 2y = 3−x+2y=3 intersect at the point (1,2)(1,2)(1,2).

We plug this solution in to the original system of equations to check our work:

2⋅1−2=0−1+2⋅2=3.\begin{align*} 2 \cdot 1 - 2 &= 0 \\ -1 + 2 \cdot 2 &= 3. \end{align*}2⋅1−2−1+2⋅2​=0=3.​

The solution to a three dimensional system of equations is the common point of intersection of three planes (if there is one).

Column Picture

In the column picture we rewrite the system of linear equations as a single equation by turning the coefficients in the columns of the system into vectors:

x[2−1]+y[−12]=[03].x\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + y\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}.x[2−1​]+y[−12​]=[03​].

Given two vectors c\mathbf{c}c and d\mathbf{d}d and scalars xxx and yyy, the sum xc+ydx\mathbf{c} + y\mathbf{d}xc+yd is called a linear combination of c\mathbf{c}c and d\mathbf{d}d. Linear combinations are important throughout this course. Geometrically, we want to find numbers xxx and yyy so that xxx copies of vector [2−1]\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}[2−1​] added to yyy copies of vector [−12]\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}[−12​] equals the vector [03]\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}[03​]. As we see from Figure 2, x=1x = 1x=1 and y=2y = 2y=2, agreeing with the row picture in Figure 2.

!session1-fig2 Figure 2: A linear combination of the column vectors equals the vector b\mathbf{b}b.

In three dimensions, the column picture requires us to find a linear combination of three 3-dimensional vectors that equals the vector b\mathbf{b}b.

Matrix Picture

We write the system of equations

2x−y=0−x+2y=3\begin{align*} 2x - y &= 0 \\ -x + 2y &= 3 \end{align*}2x−y−x+2y​=0=3​

as a single equation by using matrices and vectors:

[2−1−12][xy]=[03].\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}.[2−1​−12​][xy​]=[03​].

The matrix A=[2−1−12]A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}A=[2−1​−12​] is called the coefficient matrix. The vector x=[xy]\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}x=[xy​] is the vector of unknowns. The values on the right hand side of the equations form the vector b\mathbf{b}b:

Ax=b.A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}.Ax=b.

The three dimensional matrix picture is very like the two dimensional one, except that the vectors and matrices increase in size.

Matrix Multiplication

How do we multiply a matrix AAA by a vector x\mathbf{x}x?

[2513][12]=?\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 5 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = ?[21​53​][12​]=?

One method is to think of the entries of x\mathbf{x}x as the coefficients of a linear combination of the column vectors of the matrix:

[2513][12]=1[21]+2[53]=[21]+[106]=[127].\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 5 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = 1\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + 2\begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix}.[21​53​][12​]=1[21​]+2[53​]=[21​]+[106​]=[127​].

This technique shows that AxA\mathbf{x}Ax is a linear combination of the columns of AAA. You may also calculate the product AxA\mathbf{x}Ax by taking the dot product of each row of AAA with the vector x\mathbf{x}x:

[2513][12]=[2⋅1+5⋅21⋅1+3⋅2]=[127].\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 5 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \cdot 1 + 5 \cdot 2 \\ 1 \cdot 1 + 3 \cdot 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix}.[21​53​][12​]=[2⋅1+5⋅21⋅1+3⋅2​]=[127​].

Linear Independence

In the column and matrix pictures, the right hand side of the equation is a vector b\mathbf{b}b. Given a matrix AAA, can we solve:

Ax=bA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b

for every possible vector b\mathbf{b}b? In other words, do the linear combinations of the column vectors fill the xyxyxy-plane (or space, in the three dimensional case)? If the answer is "no", we say that AAA is a singular matrix. In this singular case its column vectors are linearly dependent; all linear combinations of those vectors lie on a point or line (in two dimensions) or on a point, line or plane (in three dimensions). The combinations don't fill the whole space.

Problem 1.3:

True or false: A 3 by 2 matrix AAA times a 2 by 3 matrix BBB equals a 3 by 3 matrix ABABAB. If this is false, write a similar sentence which is correct.

Solution: The statement is true. In order to multiply two matrices, the number of columns of AAA must equal the number of rows of BBB. The product ABABAB will have the same number of rows as the first matrix and the same number of columns as the second: A(m by n) times B(n by p) equals AB(m by p)A(m \text{ by } n) \text{ times } B(n \text{ by } p) \text{ equals } AB(m \text{ by } p)A(m by n) times B(n by p) equals AB(m by p).
