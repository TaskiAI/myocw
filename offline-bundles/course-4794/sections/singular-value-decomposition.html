Singular value decomposition

The singular value decomposition of a matrix is usually referred to as the SVD. This is the final and best factorization of a matrix:

A=UΣVTA = U\Sigma V^TA=UΣVT

where UUU is orthogonal, Σ\SigmaΣ is diagonal, and VVV is orthogonal.

In the decomposition A=UΣVTA = U\Sigma V^TA=UΣVT, AAA can be any matrix. We know that if AAA is symmetric positive definite its eigenvectors are orthogonal and we can write A=QAQTA = QAQ^TA=QAQT. This is a special case of a SVD, with U=V=QU = V = QU=V=Q. For more general AAA, the SVD requires two different matrices UUU and VVV.

We've also learned how to write A=SAS−1A = SAS^{-1}A=SAS−1, where SSS is the matrix of nnn distinct eigenvectors of AAA. However, SSS may not be orthogonal; the matrices UUU and VVV in the SVD will be.

How it works

We can think of AAA as a linear transformation taking a vector viv_ivi​ in its row space to a vector u1=Av1u_1 = Av_1u1​=Av1​ in its column space. The SVD arises from finding an orthogonal basis for the row space that gets transformed into an orthogonal basis for the column space: Avi=σiuiAv_i = \sigma_i u_iAvi​=σi​ui​.

It's not hard to find an orthogonal basis for the row space – the Gram-Schmidt process gives us one right away. But in general, there's no reason to expect AAA to transform that basis to another orthogonal basis.

You may be wondering about the vectors in the nullspaces of AAA and ATA^TAT. These are no problem – zeros on the diagonal of Σ\SigmaΣ will take care of them.

Matrix language

The heart of the problem is to find an orthonormal basis v1,v2,…vrv_1, v_2, \ldots v_rv1​,v2​,…vr​ for the row space of AAA for which

A[v1v2…vr]=[σ1u1σ2u2…σrur]A \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & \ldots & v_r \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 u_1 & \sigma_2 u_2 & \ldots & \sigma_r u_r \end{bmatrix}A[v1​​v2​​…​vr​​]=[σ1​u1​​σ2​u2​​…​σr​ur​​]
=[u1u2…ur][σ1σ2⋱σr]= \begin{bmatrix} u_1 & u_2 & \ldots & u_r \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ & \sigma_2 \\ & & \ddots \\ & & & \sigma_r \end{bmatrix}=[u1​​u2​​…​ur​​]​σ1​​σ2​​⋱​σr​​​

with u1,u2,…uru_1, u_2, \ldots u_ru1​,u2​,…ur​ an orthonormal basis for the column space of AAA. Once we add in the nullspaces, this equation will become AV=UΣAV = U\SigmaAV=UΣ. (We can complete the orthonormal bases v1,…vrv_1, \ldots v_rv1​,…vr​ and u1,…uru_1, \ldots u_ru1​,…ur​ to orthonormal bases for the entire space any way we want. Since vr+1,…vnv_{r+1}, \ldots v_nvr+1​,…vn​ will be in the nullspace of AAA, the diagonal entries σr+1,…σn\sigma_{r+1}, \ldots \sigma_nσr+1​,…σn​ will be 000.)

The columns of UUU and VVV are bases for the row and column spaces, respectively. Usually U≠VU \ne VU=V, but if AAA is positive definite we can use the same basis for its row and column space!

Calculation

Suppose AAA is the invertible matrix [44−33]\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}[4−3​43​]. We want to find vectors v1v_1v1​ and v2v_2v2​ in the row space R2\mathbb{R}^2R2, u1u_1u1​ and u2u_2u2​ in the column space R2\mathbb{R}^2R2, and positive numbers σ1\sigma_1σ1​ and σ2\sigma_2σ2​ so that the viv_ivi​ are orthonormal, the uiu_iui​ are orthonormal, and the σi\sigma_iσi​ are the scaling factors for which Avi=σiuiAv_i = \sigma_i u_iAvi​=σi​ui​.

This is a big step toward finding orthonormal matrices VVV and UUU and a diagonal matrix Σ\SigmaΣ for which:

AV=UΣ.AV = U\Sigma.AV=UΣ.

Since VVV is orthogonal, we can multiply both sides by V−1=VTV^{-1} = V^TV−1=VT to get:

A=UΣVT.A = U\Sigma V^T.A=UΣVT.

Rather than solving for UUU, VVV and Σ\SigmaΣ simultaneously, we multiply both sides by AT=VΣTUTA^T = V\Sigma^T U^TAT=VΣTUT to get:

ATA=VΣU−1UΣVTA^T A = V\Sigma U^{-1} U\Sigma V^TATA=VΣU−1UΣVT
=VΣ2VT= V\Sigma^2 V^T=VΣ2VT
=V[σ12σ22⋱σn2]VT.= V \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1^2 \\ & \sigma_2^2 \\ & & \ddots \\ & & & \sigma_n^2 \end{bmatrix} V^T.=V​σ12​​σ22​​⋱​σn2​​​VT.

This is in the form QAQTQAQ^TQAQT; we can now find VVV by diagonalizing the symmetric positive definite (or semidefinite) matrix ATAA^T AATA. The columns of VVV are eigenvectors of ATAA^T AATA and the eigenvalues of ATAA^T AATA are the values σi2\sigma_i^2σi2​. (We choose σi\sigma_iσi​ to be the positive square root of λi\lambda_iλi​.)

To find UUU, we do the same thing with AATAA^TAAT.

SVD example

We return to our matrix A=[44−33]A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}A=[4−3​43​]. We start by computing

ATA=[4−343][44−33]A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -3 \\ 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}ATA=[44​−33​][4−3​43​]
=[257725]= \begin{bmatrix} 25 & 7 \\ 7 & 25 \end{bmatrix}=[257​725​]

The eigenvectors of this matrix will give us the vectors viv_ivi​, and the eigenvalues will give us the numbers σi\sigma_iσi​.

Two orthogonal eigenvectors of ATAA^T AATA are [11]\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}[11​] and [1−1]\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}[1−1​]. To get an orthonormal basis, let v1=[1/21/2]v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1/\sqrt{2} \\ 1/\sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix}v1​=[1/2​1/2​​] and v2=[1/2−1/2]v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1/\sqrt{2} \\ -1/\sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix}v2​=[1/2​−1/2​​]. These have eigenvalues σ12=32\sigma_1^2 = 32σ12​=32 and σ22=18\sigma_2^2 = 18σ22​=18. We now have:

[44−33]=U[420032][1/21/21/2−1/2]\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = U \begin{bmatrix} 4\sqrt{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 3\sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1/\sqrt{2} & 1/\sqrt{2} \\ 1/\sqrt{2} & -1/\sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix}[4−3​43​]=U[42​0​032​​][1/2​1/2​​1/2​−1/2​​]

We could solve this for UUU, but for practice we'll find UUU by finding orthonormal eigenvectors u1u_1u1​ and u2u_2u2​ for AAT=UΣ2UTAA^T = U\Sigma^2 U^TAAT=UΣ2UT.

AAT=[44−33][4−343]AA^T = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -3 \\ 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix}AAT=[4−3​43​][44​−33​]

Luckily, AATAA^TAAT happens to be diagonal. It's tempting to let u1=[10]u_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}u1​=[10​] and u2=[01]u_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}u2​=[01​], as Professor Strang did in the lecture, but because Av2=[0−32]Av_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -3\sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix}Av2​=[0−32​​] we instead have u2=[0−1]u_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}u2​=[0−1​] and U=[100−1]U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}U=[10​0−1​]. Note that this also gives us a chance to double check our calculation of σ1\sigma_1σ1​ and σ2\sigma_2σ2​.

Thus, the SVD of AAA is:

[44−33]=[100−1][420032][1/21/21/2−1/2]\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4\sqrt{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 3\sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1/\sqrt{2} & 1/\sqrt{2} \\ 1/\sqrt{2} & -1/\sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix}[4−3​43​]=[10​0−1​][42​0​032​​][1/2​1/2​​1/2​−1/2​​]

Example with a nullspace

Now let A=[4386]A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 \\ 8 & 6 \end{bmatrix}A=[48​36​]. This has a one dimensional nullspace and one dimensional row and column spaces.

The row space of AAA consists of the multiples of [43]\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}[43​]. The column space of AAA is made up of multiples of [48]\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 8 \end{bmatrix}[48​]. The nullspace and left nullspace are perpendicular to the row and column spaces, respectively.

Unit basis vectors of the row and column spaces are v1=[.8.6]v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} .8 \\ .6 \end{bmatrix}v1​=[.8.6​] and u1=[1/52/5]u_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1/\sqrt{5} \\ 2/\sqrt{5} \end{bmatrix}u1​=[1/5​2/5​​]. To compute σ1\sigma_1σ1​ we find the nonzero eigenvalue of ATAA^T AATA.

ATA=[4836][4386]A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 8 \\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 \\ 8 & 6 \end{bmatrix}ATA=[43​86​][48​36​]
=[80606045]= \begin{bmatrix} 80 & 60 \\ 60 & 45 \end{bmatrix}=[8060​6045​]

Because this is a rank 1 matrix, one eigenvalue must be 000. The other must equal the trace, so σ12=125\sigma_1^2 = 125σ12​=125. After finding unit vectors perpendicular to u1u_1u1​ and v1v_1v1​ (basis vectors for the left nullspace and nullspace, respectively) we see that the SVD of AAA is:

[4386]=15[122−1][125000][.8.6.6−.8]\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 \\ 8 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{125} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} .8 & .6 \\ .6 & -.8 \end{bmatrix}[48​36​]=5​1​[12​2−1​][125​0​00​][.8.6​.6−.8​]

The singular value decomposition combines topics in linear algebra ranging from positive definite matrices to the four fundamental subspaces.

  • v1,v2,…vrv_1, v_2, \ldots v_rv1​,v2​,…vr​ is an orthonormal basis for the row space.
  • u1,u2,…uru_1, u_2, \ldots u_ru1​,u2​,…ur​ is an orthonormal basis for the column space.
  • vr+1,…vnv_{r+1}, \ldots v_nvr+1​,…vn​ is an orthonormal basis for the nullspace.
  • ur+1,…umu_{r+1}, \ldots u_mur+1​,…um​ is an orthonormal basis for the left nullspace.

These are the "right" bases to use, because Avi=σiuiAv_i = \sigma_i u_iAvi​=σi​ui​.

Problem 29.2:

(6.7 #11.) Suppose AAA has orthogonal columns w1,w2,...,wnw_1, w_2, ..., w_nw1​,w2​,...,wn​ of lengths σ1,σ2,...,σn\sigma_1, \sigma_2, ..., \sigma_nσ1​,σ2​,...,σn​. Calculate ATAA^T AATA. What are U,ΣU, \SigmaU,Σ, and VVV in the SVD?

Solution:

Since the columns of AAA are orthogonal, ATAA^T AATA is a diagonal matrix with entries σ12,...,σn2\sigma_1^2, ..., \sigma_n^2σ12​,...,σn2​. Since ATA=VΣ2VTA^T A = V\Sigma^2 V^TATA=VΣ2VT, we find that Σ2\Sigma^2Σ2 is the matrix with diagonal entries σ12,...,σn2\sigma_1^2, ..., \sigma_n^2σ12​,...,σn2​ and thus that Σ\SigmaΣ is the matrix with diagonal entries σ1,...,σn\sigma_1, ..., \sigma_nσ1​,...,σn​. Referring again to the equation ATA=VΣ2VTA^T A = V\Sigma^2 V^TATA=VΣ2VT, we conclude also that V=IV = IV=I. The equation A=UΣVTA = U\Sigma V^TA=UΣVT then tells us that UUU must be the matrix whose columns are 1σiwi\frac{1}{\sigma_i}w_iσi​1​wi​.
