Positive definite matrices and minima

Studying positive definite matrices brings the whole course together; we use pivots, determinants, eigenvalues and stability. The new quantity here is xTAxx^T AxxTAx; watch for it.

This lecture covers how to tell if a matrix is positive definite, what it means for it to be positive definite, and some geometry.

Positive definite matrices

Given a symmetric two by two matrix [abbc]\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{bmatrix}[ab​bc​], here are four ways to tell if it's positive definite:

  1. Eigenvalue test: λ1>0,λ2>0\lambda_1 > 0, \lambda_2 > 0λ1​>0,λ2​>0.
  2. Determinants test: a>0,ac−b2>0a > 0, ac - b^2 > 0a>0,ac−b2>0.
  3. Pivot test: a>0,ac−b2a>0a > 0, \frac{ac - b^2}{a} > 0a>0,aac−b2​>0.
  4. xTAxx^T AxxTAx is positive except when x=0x = 0x=0 (this is usually the definition of positive definiteness).

Hessian matrix

The matrix of second derivatives of f(x,y)f(x, y)f(x,y) is:

[fxxfxyfyxfyy].\begin{bmatrix} f_{xx} & f_{xy} \\ f_{yx} & f_{yy} \end{bmatrix}.[fxx​fyx​​fxy​fyy​​].

This matrix is symmetric because fxy=fyxf_{xy} = f_{yx}fxy​=fyx​. Its determinant is positive when the matrix is positive definite, which matches the fxxfyy>fxy2f_{xx}f_{yy} > f_{xy}^2fxx​fyy​>fxy2​ test for a minimum that we learned in calculus.

n by n

A function of several variables f(x1,x2,...,xn)f(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n)f(x1​,x2​,...,xn​) has a minimum when its matrix of second derivatives is positive definite, and identifying minima of functions is often important. The tests we've just learned for 2 by 2 matrices also apply to nnn by nnn matrices.

A 3 by 3 example:

A=[2−10−12−10−12]A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}A=​2−10​−12−1​0−12​​

The pivots of AAA are 2,3/22, 3/22,3/2 and 4/34/34/3 (all positive) because the products of the pivots equal the determinants.
