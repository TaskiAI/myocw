Lecture Notes

Transposes, permutations, spaces Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn

In this lecture we introduce vector spaces and their subspaces.

Permutations

Multiplication by a permutation matrix PPP swaps the rows of a matrix; when applying the method of elimination we use permutation matrices to move zeros out of pivot positions. Our factorization A=LUA = LUA=LU then becomes PA=LUPA = LUPA=LU, where PPP is a permutation matrix which reorders any number of rows of AAA. Recall that P−1=PTP^{-1} = P^TP−1=PT, i.e. that PTP=IP^T P = IPTP=I.

Transposes

When we take the transpose of a matrix, its rows become columns and its columns become rows. If we denote the entry in row iii column jjj of matrix AAA by AijA_{ij}Aij​, then we can describe ATA^TAT by: (AT)ij=Aji(A^T)_{ij} = A_{ji}(AT)ij​=Aji​. For example:

(132341)T=(124331)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 1 \end{pmatrix}^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 4 \\ 3 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}​124​331​​T=(13​23​41​)

A matrix AAA is symmetric if AT=AA^T = AAT=A. Given any matrix RRR (not necessarily square) the product RTRR^T RRTR is always symmetric, because (RTR)T=RT(RT)T=RTR(R^T R)^T = R^T (R^T)^T = R^T R(RTR)T=RT(RT)T=RTR. (Note that (RT)T=R(R^T)^T = R(RT)T=R.)

Vector spaces

We can add vectors and multiply them by numbers, which means we can discuss linear combinations of vectors. These combinations follow the rules of a vector space.

One such vector space is R2\mathbb{R}^2R2, the set of all vectors with exactly two real number components. We depict the vector (ab)\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix}(ab​) by drawing an arrow from the origin to the point (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) which is aaa units to the right of the origin and bbb units above it, and we call R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 the "x-y plane".

Another example of a space is Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, the set of (column) vectors with nnn real number components.

Closure

The collection of vectors with exactly two positive real valued components is not a vector space. The sum of any two vectors in that collection is again in the collection, but multiplying any vector by, say, −5-5−5, gives a vector that's not in the collection. We say that this collection of positive vectors is closed under addition but not under multiplication.

If a collection of vectors is closed under linear combinations (i.e. under addition and multiplication by any real numbers), and if multiplication and addition behave in a reasonable way, then we call that collection a vector space.

Subspaces

A vector space that is contained inside of another vector space is called a subspace of that space. For example, take any non-zero vector vvv in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2. Then the set of all vectors cvcvcv, where ccc is a real number, forms a subspace of R2\mathbb{R}^2R2. This collection of vectors describes a line through (00)\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}(00​) in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 and is closed under addition.

A line in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 that does not pass through the origin is not a subspace of R2\mathbb{R}^2R2. Multiplying any vector on that line by 000 gives the zero vector, which does not lie on the line. Every subspace must contain the zero vector because vector spaces are closed under multiplication.

The subspaces of R2\mathbb{R}^2R2 are:

  1. all of R2\mathbb{R}^2R2,
  2. any line through (00)\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}(00​) and
  3. the zero vector alone (Z).

The subspaces of R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 are:

  1. all of R3\mathbb{R}^3R3,
  2. any plane through the origin,
  3. any line through the origin, and
  4. the zero vector alone (Z).

Column space

Given a matrix AAA with columns in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3, these columns and all their linear combinations form a subspace of R3\mathbb{R}^3R3. This is the column space C(A)C(A)C(A). If A=(132341)A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 1 \end{pmatrix}A=​124​331​​, the column space of AAA is the plane through the origin in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 containing (124)\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}​124​​ and (331)\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}​331​​.

Our next task will be to understand the equation Ax=bAx = bAx=b in terms of subspaces and the column space of AAA.

Problem 5.3:

(3.1 #18.) True or false (check addition or give a counterexample): a) The symmetric matrices in MMM (with AT=AA^T = AAT=A) form a subspace. b) The skew-symmetric matrices in MMM (with AT=−AA^T = -AAT=−A) form a subspace. c) The unsymmetric matrices in MMM (with AT≠AA^T \neq AAT=A) form a subspace.

Solution:

a) True: AT=AA^T = AAT=A and BT=BB^T = BBT=B lead to: (A+B)T=AT+BT=A+B(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T = A + B(A+B)T=AT+BT=A+B, and (cA)T=cA(cA)^T = cA(cA)T=cA.

b) True: AT=−AA^T = -AAT=−A and BT=−BB^T = -BBT=−B lead to: (A+B)T=AT+BT=−A−B=−(A+B)(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T = -A - B = -(A + B)(A+B)T=AT+BT=−A−B=−(A+B), and (cA)T=−cA(cA)^T = -cA(cA)T=−cA.

c) False:

(1100)+(0011)=(1111)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}(10​10​)+(01​01​)=(11​11​)

The first two matrices are unsymmetric, but their sum is symmetric.
