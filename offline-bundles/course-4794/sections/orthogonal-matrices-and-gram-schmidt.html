Orthogonal matrices and Gram-Schmidt

In this lecture we finish introducing orthogonality. Using an orthonormal basis or a matrix with orthonormal columns makes calculations much easier. The Gram-Schmidt process starts with any basis and produces an orthonormal basis that spans the same space as the original basis.

Orthonormal vectors

The vectors q1,q2,…,qnq_1, q_2, \dots, q_nq1​,q2​,…,qn​ are orthonormal if:

qiTqj={0if i≠j1if i=j.q_i^T q_j = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } i \neq j \\ 1 & \text{if } i = j. \end{cases}qiT​qj​={01​if i=jif i=j.​

In other words, they all have (normal) length 1 and are perpendicular (ortho) to each other. Orthonormal vectors are always independent.

Orthonormal matrix

If the columns of Q=[q1…qn]Q = [q_1 \dots q_n]Q=[q1​…qn​] are orthonormal, then QTQ=IQ^T Q = IQTQ=I is the identity.

Matrices with orthonormal columns are a new class of important matrices to add to those on our list: triangular, diagonal, permutation, symmetric, reduced row echelon, and projection matrices. We'll call them “orthonormal matrices”.

A square orthonormal matrix QQQ is called an orthogonal matrix. If QQQ is square, then QTQ=IQ^T Q = IQTQ=I tells us that QT=Q−1Q^T = Q^{-1}QT=Q−1.

For example, if

Both QQQ and QTQ^TQT are orthogonal matrices, and their product is the identity.

Orthonormal columns are good

Suppose QQQ has orthonormal columns. The matrix that projects onto the column space of QQQ is:

Gram-Schmidt

With elimination, our goal was “make the matrix triangular”. Now our goal is "make the matrix orthonormal".

We start with two independent vectors aaa and bbb and want to find orthonormal vectors q1q_1q1​ and q2q_2q2​ that span the same plane. We start by finding orthogonal vectors AAA and BBB that span the same space as aaa and bbb. Then the unit vectors q1=A∥A∥q_1 = \frac{A}{\|A\|}q1​=∥A∥A​ and q2=B∥B∥q_2 = \frac{B}{\|B\|}q2​=∥B∥B​ form the desired orthonormal basis.

Let A=aA = aA=a. We get a vector orthogonal to AAA in the space spanned by aaa and bbb by projecting bbb onto aaa and letting B=b−pB = b - pB=b−p. (BBB is what we previously called eee.)

B=b−ATbATAA.B = b - \frac{A^T b}{A^T A} A.B=b−ATAATb​A.

If we multiply both sides of this equation by ATA^TAT, we see that ATB=0A^T B = 0ATB=0.

What if we had started with three independent vectors, aaa, bbb and ccc? Then we'd find a vector CCC orthogonal to both AAA and BBB by subtracting from ccc its components in the AAA and BBB directions:

C=c−ATcATAA−BTcBTBB.C = c - \frac{A^T c}{A^T A} A - \frac{B^T c}{B^T B} B.C=c−ATAATc​A−BTBBTc​B.

The column space of QQQ is the plane spanned by aaa and bbb.

When we studied elimination, we wrote the process in terms of matrices and found A=LUA = LUA=LU. A similar equation A=QRA = QRA=QR relates our starting matrix AAA to the result QQQ of the Gram-Schmidt process. Where LLL was lower triangular, RRR is upper triangular.

Suppose A=[a1 a2]A = [a_1 \ a_2]A=[a1​ a2​]. Then:

A=[a1 a2]=[q1 q2][q1Ta1q1Ta2q2Ta1q2Ta2]=QR.A = [a_1 \ a_2] = [q_1 \ q_2] \begin{bmatrix} q_1^T a_1 & q_1^T a_2 \\ q_2^T a_1 & q_2^T a_2 \end{bmatrix} = QR.A=[a1​ a2​]=[q1​ q2​][q1T​a1​q2T​a1​​q1T​a2​q2T​a2​​]=QR.

Notice that R=QTAR = Q^T AR=QTA. This makes sense; QTQ=IQ^T Q = IQTQ=I.

Problem 17.1: (4.4 #10.b Introduction to Linear Algebra: Strang)

Orthonormal vectors are automatically linearly independent.

Matrix Proof: Show that Qx=0Qx = 0Qx=0 implies x=0x = 0x=0. Since QQQ may be rectangular, you can use QTQ^TQT but not Q−1Q^{-1}Q−1.

Solution: By definition, QQQ is a matrix whose columns are orthonormal, and so we know that QTQ=IQ^T Q = IQTQ=I (where QQQ may be rectangular). Then:

Qx=0⇒QTQx=QT0⇒Ix=0⇒x=0Qx = 0 \Rightarrow Q^T Qx = Q^T 0 \Rightarrow Ix = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0Qx=0⇒QTQx=QT0⇒Ix=0⇒x=0

Thus the nullspace of QQQ is the zero vector, and so the columns of QQQ are linearly independent. There are no non-zero linear combinations of the columns that equal the zero vector. Thus, orthonormal vectors are automatically linearly independent.
