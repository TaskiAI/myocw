Eigenvalues and eigenvectors

The subject of eigenvalues and eigenvectors will take up most of the rest of the course. We will again be working with square matrices. Eigenvalues are special numbers associated with a matrix and eigenvectors are special vectors.

Eigenvectors and eigenvalues

A matrix AAA acts on vectors xxx like a function does, with input xxx and output AxAxAx. Eigenvectors are vectors for which AxAxAx is parallel to xxx. In other words:

Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx

In this equation, xxx is an eigenvector of AAA and λ\lambdaλ is an eigenvalue of AAA.

Eigenvalue 0

If the eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ equals 000 then Ax=0x=0Ax = 0x = \mathbf{0}Ax=0x=0. Vectors with eigenvalue 000 make up the nullspace of AAA; if AAA is singular, then λ=0\lambda = 0λ=0 is an eigenvalue of AAA.

Examples

Suppose PPP is the matrix of a projection onto a plane. For any xxx in the plane Px=xPx = xPx=x, so xxx is an eigenvector with eigenvalue 111. A vector xxx perpendicular to the plane has Px=0Px = \mathbf{0}Px=0, so this is an eigenvector with eigenvalue λ=0\lambda = 0λ=0. The eigenvectors of PPP span the whole space (but this is not true for every matrix).

The matrix B=[1001]B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}B=[10​01​] has an eigenvector x=[10]x = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}x=[10​] with eigenvalue 111 and another eigenvector x=[1−1]x = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}x=[1−1​] with eigenvalue −1-1−1. These eigenvectors span the space. They are perpendicular because B=BTB = B^TB=BT (as we will prove).

det⁡(A−λI)=0\det(A - \lambda I) = 0det(A−λI)=0

An nnn by nnn matrix will have nnn eigenvalues, and their sum will be the sum of the diagonal entries of the matrix: a11+a22+⋯+anna_{11} + a_{22} + \cdots + a_{nn}a11​+a22​+⋯+ann​. This sum is the trace of the matrix. For a two by two matrix, if we know one eigenvalue we can use this fact to find the second.

Can we solve Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx for the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of AAA? Both λ\lambdaλ and xxx are unknown; we need to be clever to solve this problem:

Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx
(A−λI)x=0(A - \lambda I)x = \mathbf{0}(A−λI)x=0

In order for xxx to be an eigenvector, A−λIA - \lambda IA−λI must be singular. In other words, det⁡(A−λI)=0\det(A - \lambda I) = 0det(A−λI)=0. We can solve this characteristic equation for λ\lambdaλ to get nnn solutions.

If we're lucky, the solutions are distinct. If not, we have one or more repeated eigenvalues.

Once we've found an eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ, we can use elimination to find the nullspace of A−λIA - \lambda IA−λI. The vectors in that nullspace are eigenvectors of AAA with eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ.

Calculating eigenvalues and eigenvectors

Let A=[3113]A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}A=[31​13​]. Then:

det⁡(A−λI)=∣3−λ113−λ∣\det(A - \lambda I) = \begin{vmatrix} 3-\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{vmatrix}det(A−λI)=​3−λ1​13−λ​​
=(3−λ)2−1= (3 - \lambda)^2 - 1=(3−λ)2−1
=λ2−6λ+8.= \lambda^2 - 6\lambda + 8.=λ2−6λ+8.

Note that the coefficient 666 is the trace (sum of diagonal entries) and 888 is the determinant of AAA. In general, the eigenvalues of a two by two matrix are the solutions to:

λ2−trace(A)⋅λ+det⁡A=0.\lambda^2 - \text{trace}(A) \cdot \lambda + \det A = 0.λ2−trace(A)⋅λ+detA=0.

Just as the trace is the sum of the eigenvalues of a matrix, the product of the eigenvalues of any matrix equals its determinant.

For A=[3113]A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}A=[31​13​], the eigenvalues are λ1=4\lambda_1 = 4λ1​=4 and λ2=2\lambda_2 = 2λ2​=2. We find the eigenvector x1=[11]x_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}x1​=[11​] for λ1=4\lambda_1 = 4λ1​=4 in the nullspace of A−λ1I=[−111−1]A - \lambda_1 I = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}A−λ1​I=[−11​1−1​]. The nullspace is an entire line; x2x_2x2​ could be any vector on that line. A natural choice is x2=[−11]x_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}x2​=[−11​].

Note that these eigenvectors are the same as those of B=[0110]B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}B=[01​10​]. Adding 3I3I3I to the matrix B=[0110]B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}B=[01​10​] added 3 to each of its eigenvalues and did not change its eigenvectors, because Ax=(B+3I)x=Bx+3x=(λB+3)xAx = (B + 3I)x = Bx + 3x = (\lambda_B + 3)xAx=(B+3I)x=Bx+3x=(λB​+3)x.

A caution

Similarly, if Ax=λxAx = \lambda xAx=λx and Bx=αxBx = \alpha xBx=αx, (A+B)x=(λ+α)x(A + B)x = (\lambda + \alpha)x(A+B)x=(λ+α)x. It would be nice if the eigenvalues of a matrix sum were always the sums of the eigenvalues, but this is only true if AAA and BBB have the same eigenvectors. The eigenvalues of the product ABABAB aren't usually equal to the products λ(A)λ(B)\lambda(A)\lambda(B)λ(A)λ(B), either.

Complex eigenvalues

The matrix Q=[0−110]Q = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}Q=[01​−10​] rotates every vector in the plane by 90∘90^\circ90∘. It has trace 0=λ1+λ20 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_20=λ1​+λ2​ and determinant 1=λ1⋅λ21 = \lambda_1 \cdot \lambda_21=λ1​⋅λ2​. Its only real eigenvector is the zero vector; any other vector's direction changes when it is multiplied by QQQ. How will this affect our eigenvalue calculation?

det⁡(A−λI)=∣−λ−11−λ∣\det(A - \lambda I) = \begin{vmatrix} -\lambda & -1 \\ 1 & -\lambda \end{vmatrix}det(A−λI)=​−λ1​−1−λ​​
=λ2+1.= \lambda^2 + 1.=λ2+1.

det⁡(A−λI)=0\det(A - \lambda I) = 0det(A−λI)=0 has solutions λ1=i\lambda_1 = iλ1​=i and λ2=−i\lambda_2 = -iλ2​=−i. If a matrix has a complex eigenvalue a+bia + bia+bi then the complex conjugate a−bia - bia−bi is also an eigenvalue of that matrix.

Symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues. For antisymmetric matrices like QQQ, for which AT=−AA^T = -AAT=−A, all eigenvalues are imaginary (λ=bi\lambda = biλ=bi).

Triangular matrices and repeated eigenvalues

For triangular matrices such as A=[3103]A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}A=[30​13​], the eigenvalues are exactly the entries on the diagonal. In this case, the eigenvalues are 333 and 333:

det⁡(A−λI)=∣3−λ103−λ∣\det(A - \lambda I) = \begin{vmatrix} 3-\lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 3-\lambda \end{vmatrix}det(A−λI)=​3−λ0​13−λ​​
=(3−λ)(3−λ)(=(a11−λ)(a22−λ))= (3-\lambda)(3-\lambda) (= (a_{11}-\lambda)(a_{22}-\lambda))=(3−λ)(3−λ)(=(a11​−λ)(a22​−λ))
=0,= 0,=0,

so λ1=3\lambda_1 = 3λ1​=3 and λ2=3\lambda_2 = 3λ2​=3. To find the eigenvectors, solve:

(A−λI)x=[0100]x=0(A - \lambda I)x = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} x = \mathbf{0}(A−λI)x=[00​10​]x=0

to get x1=[10]x_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}x1​=[10​]. There is no independent eigenvector x2x_2x2​.

Problem 21.1: (6.1 #19. Introduction to Linear Algebra: Strang)

A three by three matrix BBB is known to have eigenvalues 0,10, 10,1 and 222. This information is enough to find three of these (give the answers where possible):

a) The rank of BBB b) The determinant of BTBB^T BBTB c) The eigenvalues of BTBB^T BBTB d) The eigenvalues of (B2+I)−1(B^2 + I)^{-1}(B2+I)−1

Solution:

a) BBB has 000 as an eigenvalue and is therefore singular (not invertible). Since BBB is a three by three matrix, this means that its rank can be at most 222. Since BBB has two distinct nonzero eigenvalues, its rank is exactly 222.

b) Since BBB is singular, det⁡(B)=0\det(B) = 0det(B)=0. Thus det⁡(BTB)=det⁡(BT)det⁡(B)=0\det(B^T B) = \det(B^T) \det(B) = 0det(BTB)=det(BT)det(B)=0.

c) There is not enough information to find the eigenvalues of BTBB^T BBTB. For example:

If B=(001124)then BTB=(59917)\text{If } B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{then } B^T B = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 9 \\ 9 & 17 \end{pmatrix}If B=​012​014​​then BTB=(59​917​)
If B=(011020)then BTB=(5001)\text{If } B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{then } B^T B = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}If B=​012​100​​then BTB=(50​01​)

d) If p(t)p(t)p(t) is a polynomial and if xxx is an eigenvector of AAA with eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ, then p(A)x=p(λ)xp(A)x = p(\lambda)xp(A)x=p(λ)x. Problem 21.2: (6.1 #29.) Find the eigenvalues of A, B, and C when

A=(123045006),B=(001020300),and C=(222222222).A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 6 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 3 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \text{and } C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.A=​100​240​356​​,B=​003​020​100​​,and C=​222​222​222​​.

Solution:

Since the eigenvalues of a triangular matrix are its diagonal entries, the eigenvalues of AAA are 1,4,1, 4,1,4, and 666. For BBB we have:

det⁡(B−λI)=(−λ)(2−λ)(−λ)−3(2−λ)\det(B - \lambda I) = (-\lambda)(2-\lambda)(-\lambda) - 3(2 - \lambda)det(B−λI)=(−λ)(2−λ)(−λ)−3(2−λ)
=(2−λ)(λ2−3).= (2-\lambda)(\lambda^2 - 3).=(2−λ)(λ2−3).

Hence the eigenvalues of BBB are ±3\pm \sqrt{3}±3​ and 222. Finally, for CCC we have:

det⁡(C−λI)=(2−λ)[(2−λ)2−4]−2[2(2−λ)−4]+2[4−2(2−λ)]\det(C - \lambda I) = (2 - \lambda) [(2 - \lambda)^2 - 4] - 2[2(2 - \lambda) - 4] + 2[4 - 2(2 - \lambda)]det(C−λI)=(2−λ)[(2−λ)2−4]−2[2(2−λ)−4]+2[4−2(2−λ)]
=λ3−6λ2=λ2(λ−6).= \lambda^3 - 6\lambda^2 = \lambda^2(\lambda - 6).=λ3−6λ2=λ2(λ−6).

The eigenvalues of CCC are 6,0,6, 0,6,0, and 000.

We can quickly check our answers by computing the determinants of AAA and BBB and by noting that CCC is singular.
